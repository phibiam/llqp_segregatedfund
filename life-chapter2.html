Chapter 2 - Term Life Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - what "term" means, policyholder vs. life insured, single/joint first-to-die/joint last-to-die policies, level/decreasing/increasing death benefits, how premiums are set, renewable vs. non-renewable provisions, convertible term insurance, incontestability and suicide provisions, attained-age vs. original-age conversions, and when term insurance is the right fit. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

📄 What "term" means

Term life insurance is a contract where, in exchange for a premium, the insurer promises to pay a death benefit to a beneficiary if the life insured dies within the fixed TERM of the contract. The term is the period during which coverage is guaranteed to remain in place, as long as premiums are paid.

Typical terms: 1, 5, 10, or 20 years, or to a specific age (e.g. to age 60) are most common. Other terms like 3, 15, or 30 years may also be available depending on the insurer.

⏳ Age limits on term coverage

Most insurers will not provide term coverage past a specific age, often around 75 or 80, because the risk of death is too great. This means insurers stop RENEWING policies at around age 65 or 70, depending on the policy's term.

Cost reality: when term insurance is provided to older-age life insureds, premiums are very expensive compared to issuing the same coverage to younger life insureds.

👥 Policyholder vs. life insured

The policyholder purchases the contract (or later acquires ownership via gift or assignment) and makes all decisions about it: naming the beneficiary, paying premiums, cancelling, or assigning the policy. The life insured is the person on whose life the contract is based - if he dies during the term, the insurer must pay the death benefit.

Same person, different roles: the policyholder CAN also be the life insured, and can also be the beneficiary - in which case proceeds on death are paid to the policyholder's estate. The policyholder is sometimes called "the insured" (because he bought the policy) - this is NOT the same thing as "the life insured."

🧑 Single life policies

Most term insurance policies are single life policies - only one person is the life insured, and the death benefit is only paid if that person dies within the term.

👫 Joint first-to-die

A single amount of coverage is placed on two or more lives insured. Upon the death of the FIRST life insured, the death benefit is paid to the beneficiary (usually the surviving life insured). This is appropriate when two or more people share a debt obligation, or one is financially dependent on the other.

Cost advantage: a joint first-to-die policy on two lives is typically less expensive than two separate policies of the same coverage, since the insurer only assumes the risk of paying out ONE death benefit, not two.
Continuation option: joint first-to-die contracts usually terminate upon the first death. Some contracts let the survivor continue the SAME level of coverage on their own life under a new policy, without proof of insurability - usually exercisable within a limited window (e.g. 30 days) after the first death. Valuable if the survivor's health has since deteriorated.

🔀 Joint first-to-die vs. "combined" insurance

Joint life insurance should not be confused with combined insurance, a marketing concept where two individual policies are purchased under a single contract for administrative convenience.

⏭️ Joint last-to-die

A single amount of coverage is placed on two or more lives insured, but the death benefit is paid only upon the death of the LAST surviving life insured. This is most appropriate when what's being covered only arises upon the death of the last person - classically, a tax liability that only crystallizes when the second spouse dies (due to the spousal rollover deferring tax on the first death).

Practical reality: because estate planning usually happens later in life, and term insurance usually isn't available past 65-70 except at great cost, joint last-to-die coverage is more commonly structured as PERMANENT insurance (whole life, term-100, or universal life) rather than term.

💰 The death benefit and face amount

The death benefit is the amount the insurer pays the beneficiary if the life insured dies while the policy is in force. The policy's initial amount of coverage is called the "face amount." The death benefit may increase or decrease over time depending on the policy type.

➖ Level term

Most term policies offer a level death benefit - it equals the initial face amount regardless of when the life insured dies during the term. Most appropriate when the insurance need is not expected to change significantly over the term.

Common pitfall: many buyers default to level term simply because it's simple and familiar, without considering whether their actual needs will change over the term - potentially leaving them under-insured or over-insured at some point.

📉 Decreasing term

The death benefit decreases over the term while the premium stays level. Since the insurer's amount at risk declines over time, the premium on a decreasing term policy is lower than an equivalent level term policy with the same initial face amount.

Classic use case: mortgages. Banks commonly market "mortgage insurance" to mortgage customers - this is essentially decreasing term insurance, sold as GROUP insurance by a company affiliated with the bank. Few insurers currently offer decreasing term for individuals; it's mainly offered on a group basis.

📈 Increasing term

The death benefit increases over the term, applied at predetermined times (annually, every five years). The increase can be a fixed dollar amount, a fixed percentage, or (less commonly) tied to inflation (e.g. CPI). Premiums usually increase proportionately with the death benefit.

Limits: increases are usually restricted by a cap on the maximum death benefit (e.g. 150% of original face amount) or a cap on the number of increases (e.g. annually for 10 years). Increasing term has become difficult to find in Canada (still offered in the US) - in Canada, the same effect is usually achieved by adding a rider instead.
Key benefit: coverage increases even if the life insured's health declines - the death benefit keeps rising up to the policy cap as long as the policyholder pays the increased premiums.

💵 How term premiums work

A premium is what the policyholder pays in exchange for the insurer's promise to pay the death benefit. Except for increasing term insurance, term premiums are typically LEVEL over the term. Policyholders can usually pay monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or annually.

Premium tax: provinces and territories charge a premium tax of 2% to 5% on life insurance premiums, built into what the policyholder pays. Example: a $102 premium in a 2% premium tax province means the insurer remits $2.04 to the provincial government.

🎯 Risk classes: substandard, standard, preferred

Premiums are set on a substandard, standard, or preferred basis. Substandard premiums are higher, used when the life insured presents higher-than-standard risk. Preferred premiums are slightly lower than standard, available only to those with lower risk factors than the "standard" person (e.g. better-than-average health).

⚰️ Mortality costs and expenses

Mortality costs are what the insurer sets aside to cover the risk of death under the policy - estimated per policy by multiplying the face amount by the life insured's probability of death during the year. This probability depends on age, gender, health status, and other underwriting factors.

What premiums also fund: selling costs (marketing, agent commissions), underwriting (processing applications, medical exams), issuing/administering the policy, investigating claims, and paying death benefits. Careful expense management contributes to profitability - whether the insurer is publicly owned (shares traded on exchanges) or mutually owned (owned by its policyholders); both face the same expense categories.

🔁 Renewable vs. non-renewable term

A renewable policy guarantees the right to renew at the end of the term for another term, WITHOUT proof of insurability at renewal - usually limited to a specific age (e.g. to age 70). A non-renewable policy simply expires at the end of its term; the policyholder must apply for new insurance, risking higher premiums or denial if health has deteriorated.

Why renewable costs more: the insurer is taking on the risk of continuing coverage after the term ends, even if the life insured's health has declined - this risk is priced into a higher premium compared to non-renewable.

📋 Renewable with guaranteed rates vs. re-entry term

Most renewable policies provide a guaranteed schedule of renewal rates set when the policy is first issued - new premiums reflect the life insured's age at renewal, but follow a known, predetermined schedule.

Re-entry term (adjustable rates): the insurer reassesses health at renewal. Good health is rewarded with LOWER premiums than the guaranteed schedule; if health has deteriorated, the policyholder still has the right to renew, but at HIGHER guaranteed rates than a standard renewable policy without re-entry. The advantage is a lower initial premium - but this works best if coverage is only needed for a term or two, or if the life insured expects continued good health (e.g. strong family health history).

🔄 Convertible term insurance

Convertible term insurance lets the policyholder convert the term policy to permanent insurance (whole life, term-100, or universal life) at a future date - WITHOUT proof of continued insurability. This means lifetime protection can be acquired even if the life insured is no longer insurable by the time of conversion.

Why it costs more: convertible term is more expensive than non-convertible term, since it exposes the insurer to additional risk - the people most likely to convert are precisely those whose health has declined, making new insurance too expensive or impossible to obtain otherwise. Insurers may also restrict the age at which conversion is permitted.

⚖️ Incontestability and suicide provisions on conversion

A converted policy is usually treated as an EXTENSION of the original policy for contractual purposes - meaning the clock is NOT reset on key legal provisions.

ProvisionWhat it means
Incontestability limitationInsurer has 2 years after issuing the policy to void it if it discovers an error in a material fact on the application. After this "contestability period," the insurer can only void for proven fraud.
Suicide exclusion clauseInsurer will not pay the death benefit if the life insured dies by suicide within a specified period (typically 2 years) after the contract was issued.
Conversion benefit: by converting, the policyholder gets a permanent policy WITHOUT a new 2-year contestability period or suicide exclusion period starting over - the original issue date continues to govern these provisions.

🎂 Attained age vs. original age

"Attained age" is usually the age term premiums are based on. Depending on the insurer's administrative practice, attained age may be calculated based on age at the LAST birthday, NEAREST birthday, or NEXT birthday.

Worked example: Manuel buys a policy August 12, 2022, born November 1, 1988. His premium would be based on age 33 (last birthday), age 34 (nearest birthday), or age 34 (next birthday) - depending entirely on the insurer's specific method.

🔢 Attained-age vs. original-age conversion

An attained-age conversion bases the new permanent insurance premiums on the life insured's age AT THE TIME OF CONVERSION. An original-age conversion (or "retroactive conversion") bases premiums on the age when the ORIGINAL term contract was first issued.

The trade-off: original-age conversions result in LOWER permanent insurance premiums (advantageous), but the INITIAL term premiums for an original-age convertible policy are higher than for an attained-age convertible policy, since the insurer is taking on the risk of providing lifetime coverage at a lower future rate. Additionally, original-age conversion typically requires a lump-sum "catch-up" payment - either equal to the cash value that would have accumulated under permanent insurance from the start, or the difference between premiums actually paid and what would have been paid under permanent insurance from the start. Because this lump sum can be substantial, many insurers either don't offer original-age conversion, or restrict it to a window of 5-10 years after the policy was first purchased.

🧭 When term insurance is the right fit

As a general rule, term insurance should be considered TEMPORARY insurance, suited for risks that will end before the life insured reaches 60-70 years of age. If the risk extends until life expectancy, some form of permanent insurance should be considered instead.

Use caseExample
Short-term risks of known durationA 5-year interest-only business loan requiring $100,000 coverage naming the lender as beneficiary - a 5-year term policy is the most cost-effective match
Decreasing risksA mortgage being aggressively paid down - decreasing term insurance matches the declining amount at risk
Limited cash flowA young family with tight finances but a real insurance need - convertible term provides affordable coverage now with the option to convert to permanent later as finances improve
"Buy term and invest the difference": a strategy where the policyholder buys cheaper term insurance and invests the premium savings (vs. permanent insurance) elsewhere. Can build wealth over time, but only works if the policyholder is genuinely disciplined about investing the difference, and if those investments perform well over time - it depends entirely on follow-through, not just the initial decision.
