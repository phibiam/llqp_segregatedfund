Chapter 11 - Recommending an Insurance Policy

This chapter covers how to translate a client's identified needs into a concrete insurance recommendation. Topics include the risk assessment matrix (probability, severity, duration of risk), the income replacement approach (capitalization of lost income, adjusting for taxes and inflation), the capital needs approach (income shortfall, capital needs analysis, available assets, existing insurance, capital shortfall calculation), bringing it all together (duration of risk, investment needs, cash flow, spouse/dependent coverage), making the recommendation (type, death benefit, premiums, cumulative cost comparison, beneficiary designations), and key policy provisions to highlight (exclusions, incontestability, grace period, reinstatement, right of rescission, surrender charges). The Derek and Becky case study is completed throughout.

📊 Risk assessment matrix: probability, severity, and duration

Before recommending any insurance, the agent should assess the probability, severity, and duration of the client's risk. This information can be organized in a risk assessment matrix. The five key risk categories are: death (low probability, HIGH financial impact); disability (medium/high probability, medium/high financial impact); critical illness (medium probability, medium/high financial impact); incapacity requiring long-term care (low/medium probability, medium financial impact); and medical/dental expenses (HIGH probability, LOW financial impact).

Each person's matrix is unique and evolves over time: a healthy 30-year-old male with a young family has LOW probability of death but HIGH financial impact if it occurs. An 85-year-old single female with no dependants has HIGH probability of death but LOW financial impact.
Objective of presenting risk: not to instill fear, but to instill hope that in a worst-case scenario, life insurance can assure a best-case future for those who remain. People do not want to think of their own death, but they can think of family members and loved ones in need. An unmarried 30-year-old without close family ties is unlikely to want or need life insurance, while a 30-year-old with a spouse, two children, a disabled sibling, and aging parents might be highly motivated.

📉 Probability of death: age, gender, health history, and lifestyle

The agent should be able to convey that risk of death depends on several factors. Age and gender: the odds of dying increase with age for both males and females; females have lower odds than males until age 100. Example: a male at age 30 has odds of 1 in 893 of dying before his next birthday; a female at 30 has odds of 1 in 1,961. A male at age 80 has odds of 1 in 21; a female at 80 has odds of 1 in 30.

Personal and family health history: if the life insured has a history of health problems, or if ascendant relatives experienced conditions with a genetic component (e.g., diabetes), his probability of death is likely higher than the average Canadian. Case study: Derek's father and two uncles were diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes before age 50. Derek is overweight and predisposed to the same disease, increasing his odds of premature death.
Lifestyle risks that increase probability of premature death: smoking; a stressful job; excessive drinking; a history of driving infractions; frequent travel, particularly to developing countries; hazardous activities or hobbies. Case study: Derek has smoked for 6-7 years and still smokes 10 cigarettes per day, increasing his risk of death. Becky does not smoke. Neither has risky jobs or dangerous hobbies.
Case study death probabilities: Derek (47) has a 0.242% probability of dying before his next birthday = odds of 1 in 413. Becky (38) has a 0.067% probability = odds of 1 in 1,389. Derek's risk is substantially higher than the average, given his age, family health history, and smoking status.

💸 Financial impacts and duration of risk

The agent should help the client understand what the loss of income will mean financially. The biggest financial impacts typically relate to: cost of replacing a caregiver; repaying debts (mortgage or personal loan); income taxes from deemed disposition of capital property at death; changes to savings plans (education or retirement); and lifestyle changes (eliminating children's activities, loss of holidays).

Case study financial impacts of Derek's death: loss of $8,400/month after-tax employment income; potential calling of the $318,120 mortgage and $41,000 car loan (requiring full repayment); passing of the cottage to the children resulting in a tax liability. Financial impact of Becky's death: Derek would need to hire a caregiver for the children so he can continue working full-time.
Duration of risk matters for insurance type selection: most of Derek's financial obligations have a limited duration - spousal support ($500/month) until Susan turns 65 (21 more years); child support ($1,000/month) until Jordan turns 19 (11 more years); mortgage ($1,800/month) for 18 more years; car loan paid off in 6 more years. However, the cottage capital gains tax liability could be a concern for 40-50+ more years AND will likely INCREASE as the cottage continues to appreciate in value - making it a permanent concern best suited to permanent insurance.
Other risks to consider: risk of illness/disability (disability risk is significantly HIGHER than death risk for all ages; can have greater financial impact since the person needs specialized care but can no longer earn income - waiver of premium or dread disease riders may help). Derek has a generous employer disability policy so is not interested in these. Risk of unemployment: if income fluctuates, a universal life (UL) policy with flexible premium deposits may be beneficial.

🔄 Income replacement approach: capitalization of lost income

The income replacement approach is used when a loss of employment income would result from the death of an income earner. It determines how to replace that lost income through insurance, assuming that if income can be replaced, the surviving family will not experience a reduction in their standard of living. Where the life insured provides an essential but unpaid function (like Becky's caregiving), his contribution can be expressed as what it would cost to replace that service (income-in-kind).

Capitalization of lost income formula: Capitalized value = annual income ÷ rate of return. This converts the value of future income into today's dollars - it calculates how much capital (insurance proceeds) would need to be invested at today's rate to generate the lost income. This is the CAPITAL RETENTION method - the capital itself always remains intact and only the interest is used, so the income stream can be replicated in perpetuity.
Case study basic calculation: Derek takes home $8,400/month. At a 5% rate of return: $2,016,000 = ($8,400 × 12) ÷ 5%. This suggests about $2 million in life insurance. The capital retention approach tends to produce HIGH estimates because it assumes the capital is never touched.

🧮 Adjusting the income replacement approach: taxes and inflation

The basic capitalization formula has important weaknesses that can be corrected with adjustments.

Adjusting for income taxes: life insurance proceeds are not taxable when received, but investment income earned on the invested proceeds IS taxable. If the lost income is expressed as an after-tax amount, the interest rate used must be a NET (after-tax) rate. Formula: After-tax rate of return = rate of return × (1 – tax rate). Case study: 5% return × (1 – 25% average tax rate) = 3.75% after-tax rate. Result: $2,688,000 = ($8,400 × 12) ÷ 3.75%. For large income replacements, use the EFFECTIVE or AVERAGE tax rate (not marginal rate, which applies only for small additional income amounts).
Adjusting for inflation: an individual's employment income usually increases over time. Formula: Inflation-adjusted rate of return = [(1 + return) ÷ (1 + inflation rate)] – 1. Case study at 2% inflation, 5% return: [(1 + 0.05) ÷ (1 + 0.02)] – 1 = 2.94%. Result: $3,428,571 = ($8,400 × 12) ÷ 2.94%. At 3% inflation with 5% return: [(1.05) ÷ (1.03)] – 1 = 1.94%. Result: $5,195,876 = ($8,400 × 12) ÷ 1.94%. Even a small inflation increase dramatically raises the insurance need.
Adjusting for BOTH taxes and inflation simultaneously: After-tax, after-inflation rate = [(1 + after-tax return) ÷ (1 + inflation rate)] – 1. Case study: after-tax return 3.75%, inflation 2%: [(1 + 0.0375) ÷ (1 + 0.02)] – 1 = 1.71%. Result: $5,894,737 = ($8,400 × 12) ÷ 1.71%.
Weaknesses of the income replacement approach: assumes exchanging lost income with interest income is sufficient (ignores the beneficiary's other financial needs at death, like paying off a mortgage); cannot recognize future income developments; may assume a beneficiary receiving a large lump sum will invest it conservatively and use only interest (in practice, capital may be depleted).

🎯 Capital needs approach: step 1 - income earned by survivors

The capital needs approach is usually MORE ACCURATE than the income replacement approach because it identifies ALL income and capital needs arising from death and converts them to a capitalized lump sum. It starts by listing the surviving family's sources of income, then compares them with their expenses to determine whether a shortfall exists.

Sources of survivor income that reduce the required death benefit: employment income, pension or investment income, or government benefits. Case study: Becky currently does not work; she doesn't expect to return for at least 6 years (Derek prefers she not return until Robbie turns 18). Neither earns investment or pension income. If Derek dies, Becky would receive a CPP survivor benefit of ~$675/month; Anya and Robbie would each receive ~$265/month as children of a deceased CPP contributor (Jordan's CPP benefit would go to Susan, since she shares joint custody). Derek excludes WC benefits (no guarantee death will be work-related). Derek's non-registered portfolio generates $7,200/year after-tax, but since those assets may be needed for estate expenses, this income is NOT included in the survivor income calculation at this stage.

📋 Capital needs approach: step 2 - ongoing expenses and income shortfall

The next step involves determining what the family's ongoing expenses will be after the death of the life insured. Expenses may increase (e.g., childcare if the life insured was a caregiver), decrease (e.g., food, clothing), be unaffected (e.g., home heating), or be eliminated entirely (e.g., the life insured's personal expenses, mortgage if paid off with insurance, support obligations handled separately).

Case study modified expense table: Derek wants all debts paid in full (so mortgage $1,800 and car loan $620 drop to $0). Support obligations handled separately (spousal $500 and child support $1,000 drop to $0). Personal expenses of Derek eliminated (his life insurance premium $20 goes to $0; gas and car insurance reduce; food reduces from $600 to $500; clothing reduces from $400 to $300; sports/fitness reduces from $160 to $100). Home maintenance increases $330 → $420. Total current expenses: $8,560. Total post-death expenses: $4,150.
Income shortfall formula: Income shortfall = expenses – income. Case study: after death, survivor income = $675 (Becky CPP) + $265 (Anya CPP) + $265 (Robbie CPP) = $1,205/month. Expenses = $4,150/month. Shortfall = $4,150 – $1,205 = $2,945/month.

💡 Capitalizing the income shortfall: capital retention vs. capital drawdown

Once the income shortfall is determined, the next step is to calculate how much capital would be needed at death to fund it. Two methods exist.

Capital retention method: Capitalization of shortfall = annual shortfall ÷ investment return (ideally the after-tax, after-inflation rate). The capital itself is never touched. Case study at 1.71% after-tax, after-inflation rate: $2,066,667 = ($2,945/month × 12) ÷ 1.71%.
Capital drawdown method: for needs with a limited duration, simply multiply the annual shortfall by the number of years. Capitalized value = monthly shortfall × 12 × number of years. This assumes the capital itself will be used up over the period. Case study (18 years until Robbie turns 18): $636,120 = ($2,945/month × 12 months) × 18 years.
Drawdown for support obligations: Spousal support for Susan ($500/month for maximum 21 years until Susan turns 65): $126,000 = $500 × 12 × 21. Child support for Jordan ($1,000/month for 11 more years until Jordan turns 19): $132,000 = $1,000 × 12 × 11.

🧾 Capital needs analysis: lump-sum capital needs

Beyond the capitalized income shortfall, all other lump-sum capital needs must be identified. These include:

Final expenses: cost of burial, cremation, funeral, or remembrance services. Case study: Derek estimates $20,000 is sufficient for final expenses.
Tax liabilities: deemed dispositions of capital assets and deregistration of registered assets can create a significant tax liability due on the terminal tax return. The executor may be forced to sell assets if the estate lacks cash. Case study: Derek plans to leave the cottage in trust to the children - this triggers a deemed disposition. ACB $250,000, FMV $600,000. Capital gain = $350,000. At 50% inclusion rate, taxable capital gain = $175,000. If marginal tax rate is 45%, tax liability = $78,750. Note: this is a PERMANENT concern since the liability only arises at death and the cottage will continue to appreciate.
Debt elimination: a common objective is ensuring the estate can pay off all debts at death. Case study: mortgage $318,120 and car loan $41,000.
Estate expenses: may include probate fees (charged in all provinces and territories except Manitoba and Québec; probate fee is typically a percentage of estate assets); compensation for the executor or estate trustee (often 1% to 3% of estate assets); legal fees; accounting fees for the final tax return; asset management fees; property maintenance fees. Case study: Derek's brother has agreed to act as executor without compensation, so no estate expenses are included in this analysis.
Emergency fund: a common rule is that a family should have 3-6 months of living expenses in liquid form. Death expenses can easily consume an existing emergency fund, leaving the surviving family without a safety net. Case study: Derek and Becky currently have $50,000 in TFSAs plus $20,000 in chequing. Monthly post-death expenses are $4,150 so 6 months = $24,900 (rounded to $25,000 for the emergency fund).
Education fund: Case study: Derek wants $10,000 per child in addition to RESPs = $30,000 total for three children.
Estate equalization: when assets do not divide equally among children, insurance can equalize the estate. Example: if Derek left the cottage (worth $600,000) to Jordan only, he could buy insurance to give $600,000 each to Anya and Robbie, equalizing the treatment.
Charitable bequests and legacies: the capital needs analysis can include charitable donations, scholarships, home downpayment for children, or lump-sum legacies. Case study: Derek wants to donate $100,000 to the local hospice upon his death.
Total capital needs summary (Derek's case study): Capitalization of Becky's income shortfall = $636,120; spousal support for Susan = $126,000; child support for Jordan = $132,000; final expenses = $20,000; income tax on cottage transfer = $86,265; mortgage = $318,120; car loan = $41,000; emergency fund = $25,000; education fund = $30,000; hospice donation = $100,000. Total capital needs = $1,514,505.

🏦 Capital needs approach: assets available and existing insurance

After calculating total capital needs, the agent must identify all assets available to meet estate needs AND all existing insurance coverage, then subtract these from the total to determine the capital shortfall.

Assets available - only those that are cash, cash equivalents, or that the family is willing to sell: Case study: Becky's RRSP, Derek's RRSP, Derek's DCPP, and the house are NOT available (Becky keeps them for retirement or residence). Cottage NOT available (held in trust for children). New car kept by Becky, van sold. Available assets: TFSAs $50,000 (tax-free); chequing $20,000 (tax-free); vintage cars after capital gains tax $69,300 (gain of $12,000 × 50% inclusion × 45% MTR = $2,700 tax; $72,000 – $2,700 = $69,300); Becky's van $10,000 (no capital gain); non-registered investments after capital gains tax $253,450 (gain of $118,000 × 50% × 45% = $26,550 tax; $280,000 – $26,550 = $253,450). Total available assets = $402,750.
Existing insurance: Derek has a $250,000 individual 10-year term policy (estate as beneficiary, renewable/convertible to age 70, due to renew in 2 years at $1,125/year, then $2,625 at age 59, $6,875 at age 69). He also has $100,000 group life insurance through his employer. Total existing insurance = $350,000.
Capital shortfall formula: Capital shortfall = total capital needs – available assets – existing life insurance. Case study: $1,514,505 – $402,750 – $250,000 – $100,000 = $761,755. Rounded to nearest $10,000: Derek should acquire approximately $760,000 in additional life insurance.

⚖️ Bringing it all together: duration, investment needs, and cash flow

Once the capital shortfall is determined, a few more factors guide the specific recommendation.

Duration of risk → type of insurance: needs suited to TERM insurance: mortgage payoff (duration of mortgage, usually 25 years or less); children's needs until financially independent (~age 25); income replacement until retirement (age 55-65). Needs suited to PERMANENT insurance: special needs children who may never become self-sufficient; tax liability that only arises upon death (may be 30, 40, or 50+ years away AND likely increases as assets appreciate).
Case study term vs. permanent needs: Term needs: income shortfall until Robbie turns 18 (~18 years); spousal support for Susan (~21 years); child support for Jordan (11 years); mortgage (18 years). Permanent needs: $100,000 hospice donation (should be available regardless of when death occurs); cottage capital gains tax (permanent and increasing concern). Therefore: term insurance for the income/debt needs; WHOLE LIFE (not UL, since Derek isn't seeking additional investment and cash flow is limited) for the permanent needs.
Joint-last-to-die NOT appropriate here: while joint-last-to-die policies are often used to cover tax liabilities on family assets left to children, it is NOT appropriate for Derek since the cottage is to be held in trust for the children upon HIS death - there is no opportunity to roll it over to Becky and defer the capital gain until her death. Therefore, all of Derek's life insurance should be on a single-life basis.
Investment needs: if the client has maximized RRSP and TFSA contributions and wants additional tax-deferral or wealth transfer to the next generation, whole life or UL may be a reasonable option. Case study: Derek and Becky are NOT maximizing their RRSPs or TFSAs, so this is not currently a priority.
Cash flow vs. premiums: if cash flow is limited, the client may not be able to afford permanent insurance. A term policy with a conversion option allows him to convert to permanent coverage later when cash flow improves. If cash flow is adequate but fluctuates, a UL policy with adjustable premiums may be a good option. Case study: the family has approximately $440/month available for insurance premiums after covering the current shortfall using Derek's investment income.
Coverage for spouse and dependants: if the rest of the family lacks sufficient coverage, there may be an opportunity to cover them under the same policy (joint first-to-die or last-to-die; family life rider). Case study: Derek wants $150,000 on Becky's life and $10,000 on each of the three children.

📝 Making the recommendation: type and death benefit

Derek's $760,000 additional coverage need is broken into three categories: 10-year term needs (child support $132,000 + car loan $41,000 = ~$180,000 T-10); 20-year term needs (spousal support + mortgage + Becky's income shortfall = ~$380,000 T-20 or T-10 renewable); permanent needs (cottage tax liability + hospice donation = $200,000 whole life with PUA dividend option). PUA option on whole life provides additional accumulating coverage to hedge against the cottage appreciating further.

💰 Cumulative cost comparison: T-10 vs. T-20 vs. combination

Derek's smoking status affects his premium rates. At his current age, health, and smoking status, rates per $100,000 are: Whole life with PUA = $325/month for life; T-10 renewable to 85 = $33 first 10 years, $183 at age 57, $435 at age 67; T-20 renewable to 85 = $60 first 20 years, $540 at age 67.

Option A - Single T-10 renewable ($560,000, dropping to $380,000 after 10 years): First 10 years: $560,000 ÷ $100,000 × $33 × 12 × 10 = $22,176. Second 10 years: $380,000 ÷ $100,000 × $183 × 12 × 10 = $83,448. Total cumulative over 20 years = $105,624.
Option B - Single T-20 ($560,000, dropping to $380,000 after 10 years): First 10 years: $560,000 ÷ $100,000 × $60 × 12 × 10 = $40,320. Second 10 years: $380,000 ÷ $100,000 × $60 × 12 × 10 = $27,360. Total cumulative over 20 years = $67,680. Winner if using ONE single policy - though notice T-10 is cheaper for the first 10 years ($22,176 vs. $40,320).
Option C - Combination ($180,000 T-10 for 10 years + $380,000 T-20 for 20 years): T-10: $180,000 ÷ $100,000 × $33 × 12 × 10 = $7,128. T-20: $380,000 ÷ $100,000 × $60 × 12 × 20 = $54,720. Total cumulative over 20 years = $61,848. CHEAPEST OVERALL by ~10% compared to Option B ($61,848 vs. $67,680). This is the recommended approach when coverage needs are clearly separated into distinct time periods (10 years and 20 years).
Replacing Derek's existing $250,000 term policy: Derek resumed smoking after the policy was issued, so the old policy will remain valid at standard non-smoker rates even upon renewal (since the insurer cannot retroactively re-underwrite the policy). A new policy would be priced at smoker rates, making it significantly more expensive. Cumulative cost analysis shows keeping the existing policy is BETTER than replacing it. This illustrates why maintaining existing coverage is often advantageous - premiums are locked in at the original underwriting basis.
Coverage for Becky and children: two options: (1) standalone $150,000 T-10 on Becky + $10,000 child rider = $23.54/month total; (2) family life rider on Derek's whole life policy = $19.04/month total. The family rider is cheaper because it avoids a separate policy administration fee. Total monthly cost of ALL new recommended insurance: $200,000 whole life on Derek = $650; family rider (Becky $150K + children $10K each) = $19; $180,000 T-10 on Derek = $59; $380,000 T-20 on Derek = $228. Grand total = $956/month - which exceeds the $440 available in current cash flow.
Options when recommended coverage exceeds available cash flow: reduce coverage amount and accept that Becky may return to work before Robbie turns 18; defer the hospice donation; obtain term insurance with a conversion option for the permanent needs (cottage tax and hospice) and convert to permanent when cash flow improves; scale back other expenditures; Derek may qualify for reduced premiums if he quits smoking for at least one year.

👤 Beneficiary designations: primary, contingent, revocable/irrevocable, and probate

Primary beneficiaries receive the death benefit as long as they survive the life insured. Multiple primary beneficiaries can be named (death benefit split equally by default, or in specified amounts). For taxes owed at death, the ESTATE may be the appropriate beneficiary; for income replacement, the SPOUSE; for minor children, a TRUSTEE should be named.

Case study beneficiary structure: on the $180,000 T-10 policy, Derek could name Becky as beneficiary of $50,000 (car loan repayment) and a testamentary trust as beneficiary of $130,000 (child support for Jordan, with any remaining amount going to Becky when Jordan turns 18). A testamentary trust can also be named as a contingent beneficiary - if Derek names Becky as primary beneficiary and both die simultaneously, a testamentary trust for the children keeps the funds from falling into the estate and being subject to probate.
Revocable vs. irrevocable designations: in most Canadian jurisdictions, beneficiary designations are AUTOMATICALLY REVOCABLE unless the application specifies otherwise. EXCEPTION: in Québec, if the policyholder designates his spouse as beneficiary, it is AUTOMATICALLY IRREVOCABLE unless the policyholder specifies it is revocable when first designating the spouse. Irrevocable designations should be recommended only when: required by a spousal or child support court order; needed to protect insurance proceeds from creditors; used as part of a tax strategy for charitable giving.
Probate implications: if the estate is named beneficiary (or if no named beneficiary survives the life insured), the death benefit payment may be subject to prior probate - which incurs probate fees, delays payment, and makes the will and estate contents public record (creating greater risk that someone contests the distribution). In jurisdictions with high probate fees, it is usually preferable to name a person other than the estate as beneficiary. However, if the proceeds are needed to pay taxes or other estate expenses, the estate may still be the appropriate beneficiary despite probate costs.

🔑 Key policy provisions to highlight

When reviewing a new policy, the life agent must ensure the policyholder understands these key provisions.

Exclusions: if the policy has a specific exclusion, the policyholder must understand that if death results from that exclusion, the death benefit will not be paid. The life insured must choose: either avoid the excluded activity, or accept that no death benefit will be paid if that activity causes death. Example: Anne-Marie's policy has an exclusion for death while travelling to Ethiopia - the death benefit is paid as long as her death doesn't occur in Ethiopia or from an illness acquired there. All policies also have a TWO-YEAR exclusion for suicide - if the life insured dies by suicide within the first two years after the policy is issued or reinstated, the death benefit will not be paid.
Incontestability: during the first two years of the contract (the contestability period), the insurance company can re-evaluate its underwriting. If it discovers a material mistake in the application, it can adjust premiums, add exclusions, or void the policy. After two years, the policy is INCONTESTABLE - the insurer cannot cancel it for any reason other than missed premiums OR fraudulent misrepresentation. Reinstated policies are also contestable for the first two years FOLLOWING REINSTATEMENT.
Grace period: all policies provide a grace period for premium payments, usually 30 or 31 days after the premium due date. If payment is received within the grace period, the policy remains in force. If payment is still outstanding after the grace period expires (and no automatic premium loan feature exists on a permanent policy), the policy LAPSES. If the life insured dies during the grace period, the death benefit WILL be paid, but REDUCED by the outstanding premium amount. Note: during COVID-19, many insurers extended grace periods to up to 90 days and allowed missed payments to be spread over a year or more.
Reinstatement: most contracts allow the policyholder to reinstate a lapsed policy within TWO YEARS of the lapse date, as long as he provides proof of insurability AND pays all missed premiums plus accrued interest. A key advantage of reinstatement over buying a new policy: reinstated policy premiums are based on the life insured's age when the policy was FIRST ISSUED or LAST RENEWED (not the current age). Example: Barry bought a T-20 at 38 for $35/month. Policy lapsed at 50. New policy would cost $150/month (based on age 50). Barry reinstated at $35/month plus missed premium and interest - much cheaper. IMPORTANT: the two-year contestability and suicide exclusion periods restart from the date of reinstatement.
Right of rescission: the policyholder has a minimum of 10 days after receiving the new policy to review it and return it for a full refund of all premiums paid. This is the 10-day free-look provision.
Expiry: term policies expire at the end of the term unless the policy is renewable. The agent should ensure the policyholder understands when coverage ends and what options exist at that point.
Surrender charges (UL policies): the agent must ensure the policyholder understands any surrender charges that apply if the UL policy is cancelled or a partial withdrawal is made. Surrender charges typically only apply during the early years of UL policies.

📈 Using illustrations

Illustrations (graphs and tables generated by the insurance company's software) help clients understand recommended policies and are often appended to the life insurance application. For term policies, illustrations simply show premiums and death benefit by year. For permanent policies (participating whole life and UL), illustrations are more complex, showing projected policy dividends, mortality deductions, investment account values, and CSVs by year.

Critical limitation of illustrations: projections are NOT a prediction of guaranteed or expected performance - they are designed only to demonstrate how the policy works. Participating whole life and UL outcomes are very sensitive to even small changes in investment performance over the long life of the policy. Most illustrations now include projections for at least TWO investment return scenarios to highlight this sensitivity. The policyholder is typically required to SIGN the illustration to confirm he understands the limitations of the projections - this signature requirement is also why illustrations are submitted with the application.
