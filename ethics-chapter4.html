Chapter 4 — Rules and Principles Governing the Activities of Life Insurance Agents

Ethics and abiding by the rules of professional practice are fundamental to licensed insurance agents. This chapter covers the organizations that protect clients, the main responsibilities of life insurance agents, and the licensing process and regulations — including prohibited practices, disclosure requirements, and handling complaints.

🏛️ Organizations that protect clients

Provincial and territorial regulatory authorities: OSFI (Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions) supervises federal insurance companies for financial soundness, but is NOT responsible for licensing, regulating, or supervising insurance agent practice. Provinces and territories are responsible for: licensing insurance agents; regulating market conduct (any product or service relationship between the insurance industry and the public); and licensing insurers operating within their jurisdictions. A risk-based approach directs regulatory attention toward insurers and agents where there is a high risk of non-compliance, rather than giving all equal weight.
Key provincial regulators: BC — Insurance Council of BC (agents) + BCFSA (insurers); Alberta — Alberta Insurance Council (agents) + Superintendent of Insurance; Saskatchewan — Life Insurance Council of Saskatchewan (agents) + FCAA; Manitoba — Insurance Council of Manitoba (agents) + FIRB; Ontario — FSRA (both agents and insurers, since June 8, 2019, replacing FSCO); Québec — AMF (agents, brokers, agencies, and insurers) + CSF (discipline of life/A&S agents); New Brunswick — FCNB; Nova Scotia — Office of the Superintendent of Insurance; PEI — PEI Superintendent of Insurance; Newfoundland and Labrador — Superintendent of Insurance (governed by Insurance Adjusters, Agents and Brokers Act); Yukon, NWT, Nunavut — Office of the Superintendent of Insurance in each territory.
CISRO (Canadian Insurance Services Regulatory Organizations): an inter-jurisdictional group of regulating authorities dedicated to developing consistent standards of qualifications and practice for insurance intermediaries. Key publications include: Conduct of Insurance Business and Fair Treatment of Customers (2018, with CCIR); and Principles of Conduct for Insurance Intermediaries (April 2022). Fair treatment of customers encompasses: ethical behavior; acting in good faith; prohibition of abusive practices; developing and selling products with customers' interests first; providing accurate, clear, and sufficient information; minimizing unsuitable sales; ensuring relevant advice; fair and timely complaints handling; and protecting privacy of customer information.
FINTRAC (Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada): Canada's financial intelligence unit, created in 2000. Established and operates under PCMLTFA. Mandate: facilitate detection, prevention, and deterrence of money laundering and terrorist financing, while protecting personal information. Identity verification is required for: sale of a life insurance policy or immediate/deferred annuity for which the client may pay $10,000 or more; or for which a beneficiary may receive $10,000 or more over the duration of the annuity or policy. Identity must be verified within 30 days of creating the information record. FINTRAC defines beneficiary very broadly — covering any annuity payment or surrender transaction, in addition to death benefit payments. Social Insurance Numbers (SIN) may NOT be used to verify identity for FINTRAC purposes.
Five FINTRAC identity verification methods: (1) Government-issued photo identification method — document must show name and photo, be valid/current/original, contain a unique ID number, and name/photo must match the person. (2) Credit file method — for persons NOT physically present; requires at least 3 years of history, valid and current info, name/address/DOB must match, must be Canadian, and derived from more than one source (more than one tradeline). (3) Dual-process method — for persons NOT physically present; requires two matches from two independent credit transactions at least 6 months old, or information from two reliable and distinct sources. (4) Affiliate or member method — confirming that an affiliate or member financial entity has previously verified identity using an approved method. (5) Reliance method — relying on measures already taken by another reporting entity (RE) or affiliated foreign entity. Health insurance cards may NOT be used as ID in Ontario, Manitoba, Nova Scotia, or PEI. A social insurance card may not be used to verify identity.
FINTRAC exemptions: identity verification is NOT required for registered individual or group annuity contracts (RRSP, RRIF, LIRA, LIF, DPSP, RPP, TFSA, etc.), exempt life insurance contracts, and accident and sickness insurance contracts without a cash surrender value. However, an identity verification may still be required by the insurer for exempt plans (e.g., for age confirmation, FATCA, or Common Reporting Standard purposes).
PEPs, HIOs, and their associates: PEP (politically exposed person) and HIO (head of international organization) determination is required for lump-sum payments of $100,000 or more or remittances of $100,000 or more. Foreign PEP status is permanent. Domestic PEP and HIO status ceases 5 years after leaving office. Family members of PEPs/HIOs are also covered (spouse/partner, children, parents, parents-in-law, siblings). Close associates are persons closely connected to a PEP or HIO for personal or business reasons. A foreign PEP or their family/close associate must be considered high risk for ML/TF. For domestic PEPs and HIOs, a risk assessment must be completed to determine if high risk exists.
Suspicious transaction reporting: there is no threshold or minimum amount for reporting a suspicious transaction. Suspicious transactions must be reported to FINTRAC as soon as practicable after establishing reasonable grounds for suspicion. Suspicious transaction reports are confidential — the agent must NOT disclose to the client that an analysis was completed or a report was made. Failure to report may lead to financial administrative penalties and serious criminal sanctions. The agent is protected from legal proceedings when submitting a report in good faith. ML/TF indicators include: client refuses to produce ID; client is nervous or overjustifies; client shows more interest in cancellation than long-term benefits; transaction is unnecessarily complex; transaction is inconsistent with client's apparent financial situation.
Large cash transaction reporting: if the agent receives $10,000 or more in cash in a single transaction (or two or more amounts totaling $10,000 or more within 24 consecutive hours from the same individual/entity), a large cash transaction report must be submitted to FINTRAC within 15 calendar days. Not required if cash is received from a financial entity or public body. Large virtual currency (VC) transaction reports must be submitted within 5 working days if receiving VC equivalent to $10,000 or more.
Assuris: a not-for-profit organization that protects Canadian policyholders if their life insurance company should fail. All federally regulated insurers, and most provincially regulated companies, must be part of Assuris. If a company fails, the primary regulator appoints a liquidator who will first attempt to transfer policies to other insurance companies. Since May 29, 2023, Assuris protection levels: Death benefit — $1,000,000 or 90% of promised benefits, whichever is higher; Health expense — $250,000 or 90%; Monthly income — $5,000/month or 90%; Investments/Savings (Cash Value, Accumulated Value, Segregated Fund Guarantee) — $100,000 or 90%.
OLHI (OmbudService for Life and Health Insurance): a national independent complaint resolution organization providing clients with assistance for enquiries and complaints pertaining to Canadian life and health insurance products and services. In certain circumstances, OLHI may direct life insurance agent-related complaints to the appropriate insurers.
CCIR (Canadian Council of Insurance Regulators): an inter-jurisdictional association of insurance regulators with a mandate to facilitate and promote an efficient and effective insurance regulatory system in Canada to serve the public interest. Regulatory priorities: strengthening market conduct foundations; implementing enhanced market conduct supervisory frameworks; making supervision of intermediaries more effective; and assisting clients through targeted financial education programs.
Professional associations: CLHIA (Canadian Life and Health Insurance Association); Advocis; IFB (Independent Financial Brokers of Canada); CAILBA; FP Canada. These promote professional qualifications, continuous professional development, ethical behaviour, and fair treatment of clients.

✅ Main responsibilities of life insurance agents

The underlying principle: the strength of the insurance industry is based on members providing advice and services to the public in a competent and professional manner. The underlying principle of all insurance business is utmost good faith. Obtaining an insurance licence is a privilege that requires all agents to respect applicable legislation, codes of conduct, and contractual documents. Non-compliance can lead to stiff monetary penalties and even revocation of the agent's licence.
Core ethical values and agent conduct: acting in good faith (care, competence, integrity); managing conflicts of interest properly; refraining from unfair or deceptive practices; making full disclosure; acting in compliance with regulations; and handling complaints in a timely and fair manner.

🤝 Acting in good faith

Duty of care: it is the responsibility and obligation of life insurance agents to avoid careless acts or omissions. The client's interests take priority over the interests of the agent. Erica example: forgetting to submit a client's application after a long weekend is a failure to meet the standard of care expected of an agent.
Integrity: agents must act with integrity — being honest, trustworthy, fair, reliable, dependable, and respectful. Felicity example: proceeding with an application that the agent believed was not suitable for the client's needs (without discussing it with the client), motivated only by the commission, demonstrates a lack of integrity.
Competence: agents should only carry out business in respect to products and services for which they have the required competence. If knowledge and skills may not be sufficient, the agent has an ethical duty to ask for technical assistance from a colleague or refer the client to someone who can provide assistance. Sandra example: telling a client she could prepare a proposal for a complex back-to-back insured annuity without having the expertise to do so — she should have sought technical assistance first.

⚡ Managing conflicts of interest

Priority of client's interest: the interests of the client must be placed ahead of those of the life insurance agent. An agent must not knowingly prejudice the interests of a client for personal gain, and must not take advantage of a client's inexperience or ill health. Martha example: an agent who recommends a particular insurer because her husband works there and his sales may affect his bonus has a conflict of interest.
Disclosure of conflicts: any potential conflicts of interest, whether real or apprehended, must be disclosed and managed appropriately BEFORE the client agrees to a contract or receives advice. Patrick example: an agent who is also the majority owner of a competitor business must disclose that stake at the very first meeting with a prospective client in the same industry.
Product suitability: agents must follow appropriate client needs-based sales practices. The recommended product must be suitable to the client's needs. The agent must: perform a client needs analysis gathering all necessary facts; recommend products that meet the client's needs; and explain and document the recommended products. The needs-based sales process includes: disclosure to client; client expectations; fact finding; needs assessment; recommendations and advice; and product information.
Avoiding conflict-of-interest occupations: agents must not be engaged in any business or occupation that would jeopardize their integrity, independence, or competence. The restriction is: the individual must not be in a position to use coercion or undue influence in order to control, direct, or secure insurance business. Justin example: employment as a police officer may be incompatible with being an insurance agent due to the potential for exercising undue influence or coercion.

🚫 Prohibited and unfair practices

Tied selling: making the purchase of one product conditional on the purchase of another. This practice provides more benefit to the agent than the client. Annick example: telling a client that an RRSP loan is conditional on transferring his current RRSPs into products offered by the agent's insurer — this is deceptive and tied selling.
Churning: encouraging a client to give up one product for another (usually from the same insurer) in order for the agent to benefit from the commission. This is a prohibited practice described as an unacceptable sales practice by CLHIA. Christine example: advising a new client to buy a term annuity and then reinvest in an IVIC — earning commission on both without the client's informed consent — is churning.
Twisting: persuading a client to terminate a policy in view of replacing it with another one (generally from a different insurer). The sale does not always benefit the client — important information on the full consequences of terminating the policy is often withheld. Example 1: recommending replacement without explaining tax consequences of cancelling a policy with substantial cash value, the beginning of a new two-year contestability period, or the new suicide clause. Example 2: telling a client to surrender an annuity without disclosing that the products are different, that there are exit fees, or that the replacement product has higher investment risk.
Premium rebating: giving back or rebating a portion of the premiums. It is an unfair or deceptive act or practice to pay, allow, or give a rebate of all or part of the premium to a person insured or applying for insurance. Irving example: offering to pay the first premium as an incentive for the client to sign — this is premium rebating.
Trafficking in insurance: acting as an intermediary between a policyholder who wishes to sell a policy and a potential buyer. Prohibited in some jurisdictions (e.g., Newfoundland and Labrador). Even where not prohibited, it is discouraged — several insurers may terminate the relationship with a life insurance agent participating in it. Jamie example: arranging the sale of viatical settlements without placing business through his employer and profiting from the sales — this is unethical and illegal in some jurisdictions.
Inducing to insure: using a gift or offer of payment to convince a client to purchase insurance. Lorraine example: buying a vice president expensive wine to secure group insurance business is prohibited. Nadia example: offering a term rider free of charge if the client buys a permanent life insurance policy is prohibited.
Fronting: allowing another person (especially an unlicensed person) to solicit business and submit it to an insurer under another licensed agent's name who has not seen or does not know the client. This is improper from a consumer standpoint — if something goes wrong, it is the licensed agent who will be held accountable. Agents who have participated in fronting have not only lost their licences but also frequently been left with substantial chargebacks. Donald example: signing as agent of record for a client approached by unlicensed Abner — this risks licence revocation for Donald and may prevent Abner from ever being licensed.
Unnecessary delay in delivering policies: the life insurance agent must not hold or retain documents intended for delivery to the client. Agents must deliver insurance policies or evidence of insurance coverage within a reasonable time. Kenneth example: delaying policy delivery before going on vacation for several weeks — the client died during the delay, resulting in legal action against Kenneth.
Misrepresentation: making, issuing, or circulating any illustration or sales material, or making any statement that is false, misleading, or deceptive. John example: sending a newsletter claiming a 100% guarantee on all life payments without the insurer's knowledge — when a claim is refused, the beneficiary sues for misrepresentation using the newsletter as evidence.
Misappropriating client funds (commingling of funds): taking money received from the client for a specific purpose and fraudulently misapplying it to another purpose — this is a deceptive practice and is illegal. Agents must not deposit client money in their own accounts. Many insurers have a no-cash policy requiring cheques drawn on the client's account. Joe example: depositing client cheques (intended for an insurer) into his own bank account, then lying to clients that the funds were sent — this resulted in criminal charges for theft over $5,000.
Forgery: a criminal offence involving making a false document. Harry example: forging a terminally ill client's signature on a beneficiary change application — this is forgery. False witnessing of signatures also violates professional standards. Alessio example: signing as a witness to client signatures on an application when he had never met the client — this is submitting a false document.
Holding out improperly: an agent holding out properly must: ensure his licence is posted in a publicly visible place; hold out under the name on the licence; not mislead as to qualifications or nature of business; avoid terms indicating specialized training unless actually achieved; not claim to have associates unless at least one licensed individual with equal or better qualifications exists; and not hold out as a financial planner unless holding a recognized planning designation. Cynthia example: printing business cards with an unearned designation — this is holding out improperly and a deceptive practice.
Misusing company-provided illustrations: agents must present product information accurately, honestly, completely, and in clear language. Agents must NOT change insurer illustrations or manipulate illustration software beyond its defined parameters. Gary example: modifying an illustration by removing surrender charges to make it look more appealing — this is a prohibited practice that misinforms the client.
Defamation: it is unethical to be maliciously critical or derogatory about any colleague, agency, or insurer. George example: sending an email to all clients making malicious and derogatory comments about a competing agent, his office manager, and the company he represents — this goes against ethical standards.

📢 Making proper disclosure

General disclosure: all information submitted on a life insurance application can have a direct bearing on whether the policy is issued or rejected. Nabila example: not disclosing a client's pre-existing health condition to keep the premium down — upon the client's death, the insurer returned only premiums paid instead of paying the death benefit, leaving the beneficiaries unable to pay the mortgage.
Product disclosure (minimum required in writing): provinces and territories in which the agent is licensed to sell; the insurer(s) the agent represents; the nature of the relationship with the insurer(s); how the agent is compensated; if the agent may be eligible for additional compensation based on volume of business; any conflicts of interest; and the fact that the client has the right to ask for more information. Agents should have documentation in their client files that provides evidence that appropriate disclosure has been made.
Disclosure about replacement (LIRD): Canadian regulatory authorities have adopted a harmonized disclosure form called the Life Insurance Replacement Declaration (LIRD). Key considerations for replacement disclosure include: how policies differ; why replacement might be appropriate; risks inherent in replacement; existing insurance with cash values; and negative tax consequences. A written comparative explanation of the advantages and disadvantages must be completed with the LIRD, and copies provided to the client. Roberto example: when a client wants to expedite a replacement, the agent must take the time to discuss all pros and cons with the client before proceeding — regulations require this disclosure.
Commission sharing: before splitting commissions with another agent, the agent should ensure the person is appropriately licensed. The agent must disclose to the client that there has been or will be commission splitting and provide all relevant details. Phytos example: a real estate agent receiving 50% of the life insurance commission without the client's knowledge — this is a failure of proper disclosure.
Referrals and referral fees: referral arrangements are fees paid for each lead or prospect, regardless of whether a sale occurs. These payments cannot be contingent on a sale or a percentage of commission unless being made to another licensed individual. Conditions before paying a referral fee: the person receiving the fee must not have engaged in any insurance activities with the client; and a written disclosure must be provided to the client stating that the person is being compensated for the referral. Jonathan example: paying a flat fee per name referred with proper disclosure to the client — this is ethical.

📁 Acting in compliance with regulations

Errors and omissions (E&O) coverage: agents are required to have E&O coverage (professional liability insurance) — it provides indemnity for negligence, error, or omission. E&O coverage applies to negligence, errors, and omissions, but NOT to intentional acts, misappropriations, fraud, or criminal activities such as forgery (though some provinces mandate coverage for fraudulent acts). Agents must maintain uninterrupted E&O coverage and, if leaving the business, retiring, or selling the practice, arrange run-off insurance (extended E&O coverage).
Documenting the file: agents must keep detailed notes and correspondence of the original transactions, recommendations made, and the client's acceptance/non-acceptance of those recommendations. In the event of a complaint or lawsuit, detailed files provide proof that the agent carried out business correctly. Tuan-Anh example: when threatened with a lawsuit for misrepresentation, the agent needs all files and documents available to defend his position and must immediately notify his E&O insurer.
Contract delivery: when delivering a contract, the agent must verify that no change in the client's health status has occurred between signing and delivery. If significant changes in the client's insurability are noticed, the agent must NOT give the client the policy and must have the application updated. Jeff example: checking at delivery whether there has been any change in the client's health — if yes, a new application with updated information must be submitted.

💬 Handling complaints

Definition of complaint: an expression of dissatisfaction about the service provided by an agent. It may involve a claim for a financial loss. Complaints involving unethical behaviour or alleging violations of codified ethical standards (codes of conduct and ethics) should be taken seriously.
Complaint log: the agent should maintain a complaint log to track complaints, provide required reports, and maintain readiness for regulatory audits. The log should summarize at minimum: the agent involved; instances of complaint (written or oral); recipient of complaint; individual handling the complaint; summary and details; whether a regulatory authority is involved; whether the complaint was reported to the insurer and/or MGA; steps toward resolution; statement of resolution; and date of resolution.

🪪 Licensing process and regulations

Purpose of licensing: licensing aims to protect the public against incompetent agents, unfair or deceptive practices, and unethical activities. Life insurance agents are required by statutory law to hold a life insurance licence for each province or territory where they carry out business. An agent should only sell what he is licensed to sell and only in the province or territory in which he is licensed.
LLQP: new applicants for a life insurance licence must have completed the Life Licence Qualification Program (LLQP). Two types: Full LLQP (combines life insurance and A&S training); and LLQP Accident & Sickness (A&S only — the agent would be prohibited from selling any other type of insurance). In addition to passing the LLQP, the applicant must submit a fully completed application to the appropriate licensing authority and obtain employment or an agency contract. Any false or misleading statements on the application may result in licence revocation and administrative penalties.
What fully licensed agents can sell: individual and group life insurance policies; individual and group A&S (including disability) insurance policies; and individual and group annuities (including GIAs, segregated funds, and immediate annuities).
Out-of-province licensing: a reciprocal licensing model has been created whereby regulatory authorities in other provinces rely on the requirements of an agent's home jurisdiction. This harmonizes application forms, continuing education standards, and E&O insurance requirements, making it easier for agents to be licensed in multiple provinces.
Revoking of licence: regulatory authorities are empowered to suspend or revoke a licence for activities they deem inappropriate, unethical, or illegal — including violation of any provision of the licence; material misstatement or omission in the licence application; fraudulent act or practice; and demonstrated incompetence or untrustworthiness. Agents who have had licences suspended or revoked find it extremely difficult to obtain future trust or a new licence.
Products requiring a separate licence: property and casualty insurance (requires a separate, distinct insurance licence); securities including ETFs and mutual funds (requires a licence from the relevant securities regulatory or self-regulatory organization); and mortgage brokering (requires a licence from the appropriate mortgage broker regulator).
Products NOT requiring a separate licence: acting as a deposit broker for banks or other deposit-taking institutions does not require a licence. Acting as a financial planner does not require a separate licence — but it would be misleading for a life agent to refer to themselves as a financial planner. Agents must be careful since each jurisdiction may have different rules regarding the use of titles such as financial advisor or financial planner (e.g., Ontario's Financial Professionals Title Protection Act restricts use of Financial Planner (FP) and Financial Advisor (FA) titles).
