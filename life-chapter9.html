Chapter 9 - Application and Underwriting

This lesson covers the full application and underwriting process: the agent's role, completing the application (policy details, applicant and life insured information, insurable interest), temporary insurance agreements (TIAs), the underwriting tools insurers use (APS, medical exams, MIB, MVR, inspection reports), risk classes and their premium impact, client and company factors that affect premiums, reinsurance, policy delivery and acceptance, and the group life underwriting process including creditor insurance and post-claim underwriting.

👁️ The agent's role in underwriting

A life insurance agent is NOT responsible for personally assessing the risk presented by the applicant - that is the underwriter's job. The agent's role is to help collect the information the insurance company needs for its risk assessment. The agent is often described as the "eyes" of the life insurance company, since he is often the only insurer representative who actually sees the applicant (or life insured) in person - though today that contact may be virtual.

Agent's duties when completing the application: ensure information is complete and accurate; ensure the applicant understands the consequences of providing incomplete or false information; confirm the identities of the applicant and life insured (by examining ID such as a driver's licence or passport); witness signatures on the application; and promptly submit the completed application with any premiums to the insurer.
Modernization: many applications can now be submitted electronically. Meetings often happen virtually via teleconferencing. Electronic signatures and electronic policy delivery are now allowed. Some underwriting steps are automated or AI-assisted.

📄 The underwriting process: overview

Once the agent forwards the completed application to the insurance company, the underwriter evaluates the risk. The underwriter starts with a baseline score (e.g., 100) representing the average standard person's mortality risk. For a long list of medical, financial, and lifestyle criteria, the underwriter adds or subtracts from this score based on whether the life insured presents higher or lower mortality risk than the standard person. The cumulative score assigns the life insured to a risk class.

Standard vs. non-standard applications: if the life insured's medical/non-medical evaluation and coverage amount fall within standard parameters, the policy may be issued in as little as a few days. If details fall outside standard parameters, the underwriter seeks additional information before deciding.

📋 Policy details section of the application

The application first gathers basic information about the coverage sought. Key fields include: the applicant/policyholder (legal name, address, contact information; may also include a contingent/successor owner); the life insured (specific person named, or two persons for a joint-life policy specifying first-to-die or last-to-die); the beneficiary (one or more people or legal entities; a contingent beneficiary can also be named; beneficiary can be changed after issue unless IRREVOCABLE - an irrevocable designation cannot be changed without the beneficiary's written permission, sometimes used for court-ordered support obligations or tax planning).

Other policy detail fields: type of policy (term, participating, non-participating, UL); for UL - cost of insurance type (YRT or level) and death benefit type; riders and supplementary benefits (often only addable at policy issue); premium payment options (annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly PAC); and for participating whole life - dividend payment option (premium reduction, PUAs, term insurance).
Illustrations: for UL and whole life policies, most insurance companies require the agent to submit an illustration of the policy (tables or graphs showing different policy performance scenarios) along with the application. The applicant's signature confirms he has been shown how the policy works.

💼 About the applicant: financial ability and insurable interest

The underwriter examines the applicant's: financial ability (occupation, employer, employment income, additional income sources, net worth - to confirm the applicant can afford the premiums); insurable interest (if not the life insured); justification for the coverage amount; and insurance application history (existing coverage and in-progress applications on the same life insured).

Insurable interest: the applicant must have an insurable interest in the life insured at the time of policy issue - meaning the applicant must expect to suffer a financial loss (or fail to make a financial gain) if the life insured dies. A person always has an insurable interest in: his own life; his child or grandchild; his spouse; any person he is wholly or partially dependent on for support or education; his employee; and any person in whose life he has a moral or pecuniary interest. If insurable interest doesn't exist at issue, the contract is generally void. Crucially, insurable interest only needs to exist at the time of policy issue (except for assignment); if the relationship later changes and insurable interest disappears, the contract remains valid.
Worked example - Isabelle and Justin: Justin bought a policy on Isabelle's life when they were married. After their divorce, he no longer has an insurable interest - but the existing policy remains valid. He just can't buy NEW coverage on her life. Insurable interest exception: if the life insured consents in WRITING to insurance being placed on his life, no insurable interest is required. The life insured usually confirms this by signing the application.

💡 Justification of coverage amount

Insurance is intended to protect against financial loss from the life insured's death, restoring the policyholder to approximately the same financial position as before that death. It is NOT intended to provide a way of profiting from death. Underwriters assess whether the coverage amount is reasonable given the circumstances.

Worked example - Frank/Susan vs. Jack/Jolene: both Susan and Jolene applied for $7.5 million of coverage on their husbands. For Jolene's husband Jack (earns $40,000, no children, rents, no major debts), $7.5M would likely be considered excessive. For Susan's husband Frank (earns $250,000, three young children all under 5, $350,000 mortgage), $7.5M is likely considered reasonable given his income, their debt, and their dependants' ongoing support needs.

🩺 About the life insured: personal and medical information

The application gathers detailed personal information about the life insured, including: name (current and former), date of birth, address, SIN, country of birth, nationality/residence status, employer and occupation, and bankruptcy history. Lifestyle questions cover avocations (extreme sports, adventure travel, dangerous hobbies), past and future travel, smoking habits (tobacco, cigars, cigarillos, marijuana/hashish, tobacco substitutes, cessation products), alcohol consumption, recreational drug use, driving history (violations, charges, impaired driving), and criminal history.

Medical information: height/weight (and recent changes); name of personal physician and date/reason/outcome of last consultation; whether the life insured has experienced, been treated for, or been investigated for a long list of medical conditions; current medications; other current treatments (physiotherapy, chiropractic, psychologist); parents'/siblings' health history for specified conditions; and for female applicants, pregnancy history. If an applicant answers "Yes" to a question expecting "No," they are asked for additional details.

⚠️ Incomplete or erroneous information: three situations

Agents must encourage applicants to complete the application honestly and warn them of consequences for not doing so, including voiding the contract. Three main problem situations:

1. Mistake (honest error): an inadvertent error without intent to deceive. A fact is MATERIAL if, had it been disclosed properly, it would have affected the underwriting decision (e.g., the underwriter may have declined the application or charged a higher premium). Example: listing 1954 instead of 1945 as birth year = material mistake (age significantly affects mortality risk). Forgetting to list a flu shot = NOT material (unlikely to impact underwriting). If a mistake is material and discovered within the first TWO YEARS after issue or reinstatement, the insurer can void the contract. After two years, the policy is INCONTESTABLE - the insurer cannot void the contract unless it can PROVE fraudulent misrepresentation.
2. Fraudulent misrepresentation: intentionally providing false information or failing to disclose important information to mislead the insurer and obtain a policy (or lower premiums) for which the applicant is not qualified. Unlike a material mistake, the insurer CAN void the contract even AFTER the two-year contestability period. Worked example - Eric: intentionally hid his parasitic illness from Peru. Died of liver failure three years later. The insurer voided the policy and denied the death benefit even though the two-year period had passed. Smoking is a common area for fraudulent misrepresentation: if discovered while the life insured is still alive (more than two years after issue), the insurer can void the policy and return premiums, or adjust premiums/death benefit to reflect true smoking status. If the life insured has already died (more than two years after issue), the insurer can DENY the claim.
3. Incomplete information: if the agent submits an application with unanswered questions, the underwriter returns it and requires the missing information. This delay leaves the policyholder unprotected until resolved.

💬 Agent's comments section

The application usually includes space for the agent to record anything he noticed during the process or that might impact the underwriter's assessment - including his knowledge about the stability of the applicant's employment, his own observations about the health or smoking status of the life insured (especially if those observations contradict the applicant's recorded answers), and his opinion on the truth or accuracy of the applicant's responses.

⏱️ Temporary Insurance Agreement (TIA)

Underwriting can take several weeks or months (especially if additional information or a medical exam is needed). To protect the applicant during this period, the insurer may grant the agent authority to provide a Temporary Insurance Agreement (TIA).

Requirements for TIA coverage: the applicant must submit a completed application AND at least one month's premium. Most insurers also require the applicant to complete a separate TIA form and answer "No" to a short list of health-related questions. If the answer to ANY TIA question is "Yes," the TIA is automatically DENIED. The life insured generally must also meet age restrictions (e.g., between 15 days old and 70 years old).
Coverage limits: usually limited to the LESSER of a fixed amount (e.g., $250,000 or $500,000) or the amount of coverage being applied for. TIA coverage is subject to the same terms and conditions as the policy being applied for, including the suicide exclusion (the TIA death benefit will NOT be paid if the life insured dies by suicide).
Coverage duration: the TIA remains in effect until the EARLIEST of: its expiry date (usually 60 or 90 days); the date the policy becomes effective; or the date the insurer notifies the applicant that his application has been denied and returns the premium. The underwriter may also REVOKE the TIA by advising the applicant in writing and returning the premium if additional information is required.
Agent's responsibilities: the agent should only provide a TIA if he has NO concerns about the application or the likelihood of issuance, AND the applicant submitted the first premium. Worked example - Arlene: diabetic, high blood pressure, smokes regularly, persistent cough. Agent Richard should NOT provide a TIA given these significant health concerns.

📑 Underwriting tools: APS and medical exam

The underwriter has several tools to gather additional information beyond the application itself.

Attending Physician's Statement (APS): if the application indicates the life insured has experienced a specified medical issue, the underwriter may request an APS from the life insured's doctor. The application normally includes an acknowledgement giving the insurer permission to contact the doctor directly. The INSURER pays any doctor's fees. The APS provides: a summary of the life insured's medical history, a description of current health/medications/treatments, and a prognosis for ongoing issues.
Medical exam: requested when the underwriter has continuing concerns after the APS, or when the amount or age exceeds certain thresholds. The exam can include blood, urine, or saliva tests, ECGs, and more. Usually performed by a health practitioner at an independent paramedical company. For high-value policies or complex medical concerns, a licensed physician or special tests (e.g., cardiac stress test) may be required. The INSURER pays all exam fees regardless of who performs it.

🗃️ Underwriting tools: MIB and MVR

Medical Information Bureau (MIB): a membership organization enabling life insurance companies (in Canada and the US) to exchange medical information about applicants. When someone applies at a MIB-member company, they must be INFORMED about the MIB and must SIGN A RELEASE authorizing the insurer to search the MIB database and share applicable details from their current application. When processing an application, the underwriter is required to REPORT significant underwriting issues to the MIB. MIB compiles information in coded reports covering medical conditions AND non-medical conditions (hazardous hobbies, adverse driving records). The MIB cannot itself be used as the sole basis for declining or rating an application - the underwriter must first VERIFY that the reported problem actually exists through additional investigation.

Worked example - Ernie: declined by ABC Insurance after disclosing heart problems. Moved provinces and applied at XYZ Insurance, hiding his heart history and falsely claiming to be an occasional smoker. XYZ's underwriter searched the MIB, found ABC's earlier report about Ernie's heart problems and heavy smoking, and required full medical records and a comprehensive medical exam. Based on those results, XYZ also declined his application - illustrating how the MIB prevents applicants from hiding prior disclosures by simply changing insurers or provinces.
Motor Vehicle Record (MVR): requested when the applicant fails to provide driving history details, or when coverage exceeds a certain level. Obtained from the provincial motor vehicle and licensing authority, covering 3 to 10 years depending on the jurisdiction. Reports: speeding and other moving violations, chargeable accidents, DUI charges, license suspensions or revocations, and point accumulations. Even seemingly minor infractions (failure to use a seat belt, using an electronic device while driving, running a red light, speeding) can result in a RATED policy with higher premiums. The driving record directly influences risk of death statistically.

🔍 Underwriting tools: inspection reports and financial underwriting

Inspection report: if the underwriter still has concerns, he may hire a service company to investigate the life insured and prepare an inspection report. Most inspections are completed via telephone; for large coverage amounts, a personal visit may be required. An inspection report focuses on NON-MEDICAL issues: habits (smoking, alcohol), finances (income, net worth), occupation (employment confirmation, nature of duties), driving record, and avocations (extreme sports, hazardous travel).

Financial underwriting: assesses the applicant's financial situation and the reason for obtaining life insurance, to determine if the coverage amount is reasonable for the need and if the applicant can afford premiums. Financial underwriting guidelines typically specify maximum coverage based on purpose and age of the life insured. Examples: income replacement coverage might be capped at 30 times earned income for ages 16-30, dropping to 10 times earned income for ages 60-69; estate preservation coverage might be tied to the life insured's net worth; coverage on a university student might be tied to the intended occupation.

🌍 Non-Canadian citizens and special underwriting situations

Permanent residents: generally eligible for the same coverage as Canadian citizens. Additional requirements may include: confirmation of permanent resident status; if lived in Canada less than one year - detailed bloodwork and medical exam; if frequent or extended trips to country of origin - may be rated (higher premiums) or have an exclusion for death occurring in that country.

Awaiting permanent residency: may or may not be eligible, depending on their situation and the insurer's guidelines. Some insurers set maximum coverage based on skill level: highly skilled/managerial occupations (doctors, lawyers, engineers) up to $10M; trade-level occupations (electricians, plumbers) up to $2M; live-in caregivers (nannies, support workers) up to $250K; dependent spouses or children may qualify for a percentage of the life insured's coverage (e.g., 50%). Those lived in Canada less than one year may require additional medical screening, work permit, proof of permanent residency application, and proof of earned income pattern.
International students: typically NOT eligible to purchase life insurance in Canada, unless they also fall into the permanent resident category.
Frequent travellers: the insurer may request details about past and future travel (countries visited, frequency, duration). Result may be a substandard risk rating (higher premiums) or an exclusion for travel to specific countries.
Avocations (hazardous hobbies): activities such as parasailing, back-country snowboarding, mountain climbing, scuba diving, race car driving, travel to conflict-prone countries, and piloting a private aircraft. Underwriters collect additional info about frequency, location, experience/certification, and accident history. May result in a policy exclusion for that activity, a rating (higher premiums), or even a declined application.

🤖 Accelerated underwriting vs. simplified issue

Accelerated underwriting uses technology, existing data, computer algorithms, and AI (including MIB, MVR, credit history, and other available data) to evaluate applications quickly. Unlike simplified issue, accelerated underwriting is a FULLY UNDERWRITTEN process - it can result in policies issued at or close to fully underwritten retail rates, including preferred AND rated classes.

Key distinction - simplified issue vs. accelerated underwriting: simplified issue uses a short application and instantly available evidence to make an accept-or-reject decision without human or detailed medical underwriting. It does NOT allow for preferred rates (good health) or rated policies (increased risk). Its "no-exam" premiums tend to be HIGHER than fully underwritten policies. Accelerated underwriting, by contrast, assesses applications without traditional medical exams and lab testing, but still results in a fully underwritten decision - including the possibility of preferred rates and rated classes.

📊 Risk classes and their impact on premiums

After evaluating all risk factors, the underwriter assigns the life insured to a risk class. Each company has its own system, but general categories are:

Standard risk: represents the average person. Premiums are set at the standard rate.
Preferred risk: exceptionally low risk. Usually requires additional medical screening. Eligible for LOWER preferred rates.
Rated risk: above-average but still acceptable risk. Premiums are HIGHER than standard rates, sometimes substantially higher. Hazardous occupation will typically result in a rating. Hazardous avocations are often addressed by an exclusion, but may also result in a rating, and may occasionally result in a declined application.
Exclusion: when a specific factor concerns the insurer but doesn't disqualify the entire application, the insurer may issue the policy at the appropriate risk class but exclude coverage for death arising from that specific cause. Worked example - Michael: exceptional health, would normally qualify for preferred rates, but visits Lebanon regularly (a country experiencing armed conflict). Insurer issues a preferred-rate policy with an exclusion for travel to Lebanon. If Michael dies while in Lebanon, no death benefit is paid.
Changing the risk class: once a policy is issued, the insurer CANNOT reassess the life insured at a later date, add a rating, or charge higher premiums if his health has declined - it cannot downgrade the risk rating. However, if a rated policy's REASON FOR RATING no longer exists, the policyholder may request removal or reduction of the rating (with a corresponding decrease in premiums). Worked example - Bill: was a commercial fisherman (hazardous occupation, rated policy). Left the fishing industry to work as a school janitor. Bill should ask the insurer to remove the hazardous occupation rating, as it no longer applies.
Declined: represents an uninsurable risk. May be temporary (underwriter specifies when reapplication is possible) or permanent (no further reconsideration regardless of time).

📈 Client factors that affect premiums

Several client-specific factors influence how premiums are calculated.

Age: one of the key premium determinants. Risk of death during any specific year increases with age, so premiums at issue increase as age at issue increases. The increase is NOT linear - risk grows somewhat exponentially, particularly after age 60 or 65. Attained age is the age actually reached on the life insured's last birthday. Some insurers use the age on the next birthday or the closest birthday instead.
Gender: women statistically outlive men, so a man's risk of death at any specific age is greater than a woman's. Premiums for males are generally HIGHER than for females of the same age. For transgender or non-binary individuals: insurers may underwrite based on preferred gender, gender assigned at birth, preferred gender only after transition surgery or hormone therapy, or they may use unisex (blended) rates. Previous gender confirmation surgery should not disqualify an applicant, but planned surgery will likely cause a postponement of the application until after surgery. Non-binary and transgender individuals should consider shopping around for the best rates.
Health status/risk class: preferred, standard, and rated classes each carry different premiums. Smoking status is usually reflected by an above-standard rating. Hazardous occupation results in a rating (higher premiums). Hazardous avocations are usually addressed by an exclusion, but may also result in a rating or even a declined application.

🏢 Company factors that affect premiums

Insurance companies may adjust premiums on NEW policies to address changes in three areas. Mortality costs: the amounts the insurer considers necessary to pay out death benefits. If actual mortality costs exceed anticipated amounts, premiums on new policies may increase. Administration costs and expenses: include selling costs (marketing, commissions), underwriting (processing applications, medical exams), issuing and administering policy documents, investigating and processing claims. Increasing expenses put upward pressure on premiums for new policies. Investment returns: insurers invest policy reserves to earn income that offsets mortality costs and expenses. When current or expected investment returns decrease, premiums on new policies generally increase to compensate.

🔀 Reinsurance

Insurance companies set an upper limit on the coverage they are willing to assume for any single individual - this is the RETENTION LIMIT. An insurer may still accept applications exceeding its retention limit, but only if it can pass some of that risk to one or more REINSURANCE companies. The insurer essentially insures its risk of having to pay a death benefit above its retention limit.

Worked example - John and Insure4life: John applies for $15M. Insure4life's retention limit is $10M. They contact Resure Co. (a reinsurance company) to underwrite the additional $5M. Insure4life issues the $15M policy; John pays the full premium directly to Insure4life. Insure4life pays a premium to Resure. If John dies: Insure4life pays John's beneficiary the full $15M, then receives a $5M death benefit from Resure to cover that excess amount.

📬 Issuing and delivering the policy

Underwriting continues right up until delivery and acceptance of the policy. For accelerated underwriting policies, delivery may happen electronically with a text message notification. For traditionally underwritten policies NOT delivered electronically, the agent reviews the contract in detail with the client, verifying coverage matches what was applied for, and explaining key provisions: grace period, incontestability, and suicide exclusion.

Pre-delivery check: before completing delivery, the agent should obtain verbal and written confirmation that no material change in the life insured's insurability has occurred since the application date. Changes the agent must check for include: health, occupation, recreational activities, and financial status. If the applicant CANNOT confirm no changes, or if the agent suspects a material change despite the applicant's assurance, the agent must NOT complete delivery. The agent should contact the underwriter for further consideration and advise the client that the contract is not yet in force.
Right of rescission (10-day free-look): after receiving the policy, the policyholder must sign and date an acknowledgement of receipt and acceptance (can be done electronically). The policyholder then has a minimum of 10 days to review the policy and, if desired, return it for cancellation and a FULL REFUND of all premiums paid. This is the right of rescission, also known as the "10-day free-look provision." Note: in Québec, policy delivery is not a statutory condition of entry into force, and the no-material-change requirement pertains to the period between signing the application and its acceptance by the insurer.

👥 Group life insurance underwriting

Group life insurance provides base coverage to all members regardless of their individual health, because underwriting is NOT done on an individual basis for the base coverage. However, the group plan itself still goes through an underwriting process, examining the demographics of the group as a whole (mix of ages, genders, occupations). The premium is quoted as a rate per $1,000 of coverage and applies to every person in the group or class regardless of individual age, gender, smoking status, or health status. The insurer recalculates premiums annually to reflect changes in group demographics over time.

Additional coverage: members wanting extra coverage beyond the base amount are usually required to provide evidence of insurability and are assessed individually. Premiums for additional coverage are based on the member's age and smoking status (unlike the flat group rate for base coverage).
Creditor life insurance: a type of group life insurance where the life insured usually must provide at least basic evidence of insurability - typically just a few health questions. A triggering "Yes" answer prompts further medical underwriting. If all responses are correct, the application may be automatically approved. Bank employees cannot help applicants complete creditor insurance applications (they are not licensed insurance agents). Premium structures are typically a fixed monthly rate per $1,000 of outstanding balance (common for credit cards) or per $1,000 of original balance (common for mortgages), with different rates for different age groups.
Post-claim underwriting: a significant drawback of group creditor insurance. The policy is issued with little or no underwriting (just a few yes/no questions), with thorough underwriting performed only AFTER the life insured has died. This can result in a claim being denied if the post-claim review determines the life insured did not actually qualify. If the person was simply not eligible, premiums are usually refunded - but the insured person's family receives nothing. Since the coverage is only denied upon death, there is no opportunity to seek other coverage afterward. This creates genuine concerns about the surety of coverage under creditor insurance.
