Chapter 3 — Life Insurance, A&S Insurance and Annuity Claims

This chapter covers the rules pertaining to claims and payment of benefits — who can make a claim, what evidence is required, how deaths are proven, how disputes are resolved, and the specific rules for life insurance, A&S, and annuity/pension claims.

📋 The claims process overview

When a policy matures or the life insured dies, the claimant must satisfy certain requirements for the insurer to pay the benefit: provide satisfactory evidence that the life insured has died; and prove their entitlement to claim the benefit.
The agent's role in the claims process depends on the insurance company involved. No claims process is identical. If the agent has not asked all the correct questions and gathered all the correct information in the application process, the claim could be contestable.
Claimants may include: the insured; the insured's estate; one or more designated beneficiaries (primary or contingent); an assignee of the insurance policy or death benefit; or in specific situations, a creditor (where a court order allows a claim for spousal or child support). In the event of a dispute, the insurer may pay the benefit into court.
Insurer's records: insurers will pay benefits based on the most recent beneficiary designation they have on file, or to the estate if there is no beneficiary designation. Insurers are protected by law when doing so. A designation not filed with the insurer can still be valid against a third party (except for making an irrevocable designation).
Notice of claim: the insurer usually receives notice of death from the agent, the estate executor or trustee, a beneficiary, or the employer. The insurer is not required to notify the beneficiaries upon death, but will contact the agent if the deceased used one. The insurer will record the notice and wait to receive a claim. Upon receiving notice, the insurer stops charging premiums. If the policy has lapsed due to non-payment since the insured person disappeared, the insurer will require proof that death occurred while the policy was in force.

🔍 Proof of claim — documents required

Documents required for life insurance claims: proof that the insured person has died; proof of the insured person's age; proof that the claimant has a right to receive the benefit; and proof of the claimant's name, age, and identity. A beneficiary must complete a claim form, which agents can obtain from the insurer.
Death certificate: death is usually confirmed with a funeral director's declaration of death or a provincial death certificate. If the insured person died in suspicious circumstances or overseas in a country where proof of death is questionable, more evidence may be required.
Proof of age: usually established when applying for insurance — if satisfactorily established by the agent when the policy was issued, the insurer will use the age indicated in the file when a claim is made without requiring further proof. For term coverage, proof of age can be critical since coverage expires at a certain age.
Proof of identity: the insurer will seek satisfactory proof of identity of named beneficiaries. Persons with common names, beneficiaries who have changed their name (e.g., following marriage), or beneficiaries described by relationship or representative status (children, court-appointed trustee, executor, guardian of minor's property) must provide appropriate documentary evidence.
Probate: while not required in all cases, insurers must receive sufficient evidence of the parties' right to receive payment.

⚠️ Accidental vs. natural causes

Most life insurance policies have very few restrictions about the cause of death. However, if they do have limitations (such as a suicide clause), the insurer or agent may investigate to determine if they apply.
Accidental death policies require proof that death was associated with an accident and not excluded under the policy. These policies typically require that for death to be accidental, it must be occasioned solely through violent, external, and accidental means.
The definition of "accident" has evolved through case law. The Ontario Court of Appeal determined that a person who was bitten by a mosquito carrying the West Nile virus and became a paraplegic suffered an accident as defined by the insurance policy and was entitled to compensation.
Luis example (key exam scenario): Luis suffered a sudden, massive heart attack while driving, causing his motorcycle to veer off the road. Forensic evidence confirmed no braking or turning. The coroner determined he died of natural causes (the heart attack) before the motorcycle accident. His life insurance paid a benefit, but his beneficiary was NOT entitled to the additional accidental death benefit — because he died of natural causes, not from an accident.
The insurer may require a more detailed physician's statement of death, or even a coroner's autopsy or toxicology report, to investigate accidental death claims or deaths involving policy exclusions.

👻 Disappearance and presumption of death

Seven-year rule: if a person has disappeared and remains missing for seven years or more, interested parties (most often the family) can ask a court to declare them dead. A court may make an order declaring that such person has died, usually to obtain life insurance proceeds.
Namita example: Namita's plane was destroyed by an explosion over the Atlantic. Airline records confirmed she had boarded. A judge concluded she died the day the plane crash occurred. Since she was declared dead by a court, her life insurance policy benefit became payable as of the date of the plane crash.
Timing of death is important: if a policy has lapsed due to non-payment of premiums since the insured person disappeared, the insurer will require proof that death occurred while the policy was in force.

👨‍👩‍👦 Death of two or more people

When two or more people die at the same time, the time and sequence of deaths may be important to establish who is entitled to insurance proceeds. Joint life insurance policies insure more than one person and pay upon the death of either the first or the second insured person.
Peter and Magda example: Peter and Magda both died from carbon monoxide poisoning. Based on medical testimony, the judge concluded Peter (a smoker with respiratory problems) probably died first due to more rapid breathing. Peter and his sister were joint beneficiaries of Magda's life insurance. Since Peter predeceased Magda, his sister inherited the entire death benefit. If Peter had outlived his mother, he would have been entitled to his half-share, which would then have gone through his estate.
Payment into court: if the insurer has trouble determining who the beneficiaries are (e.g., conflicting claims), provincial and territorial insurance legislation allows the insurer to pay the benefit into court. This releases the insurer from liability and allows a court system to resolve the conflicting claims. Payment into court is also required in some provinces to protect minor beneficiaries' funds.

💵 Time to pay and payout options

Time to pay a life insurance claim: the insurer is obligated by provincial and territorial insurance law to pay a life insurance claim within 30 days of receiving evidence that satisfies that the claim is payable. For individual accident and sickness insurance, the delay is 60 days. While under the terms of the policy there is no entitlement to interest on an unpaid claim, many insurers in practice pay interest for at least some of the time after death and before the benefit is paid.
Payout options: life insurance policies may provide several settlement options. The most common is a lump-sum cheque to the named beneficiary or the estate executor. Beneficiaries may also be asked which payment option they prefer when completing the claim form.
Retirement products: discussion on payout options does not apply to retirement products, which may be subject to specific transfer or payout rules. Amounts that are "locked-in" for retirement purposes must be limited to transfer options entitled under applicable legislation.
Segregated funds: the death benefit under a segregated fund (IVIC) must be calculated after death. Death benefits under these contracts can go up or down in value depending on market performance and other variable factors.

🚫 Denial of claim

Four reasons an insurer may refuse to pay a death benefit claim: insurance fraud; payment of a death benefit which is against public order; lapse (default of payment) of the insurance policy; and the claim was not made in a timely fashion according to applicable provincial limitation period legislation.
Public order (key exam concept): when a beneficiary causes the death of the life insured, it is considered a violation of community standards or public order, enabling the insurer to refuse payment. CRITICALLY: it does not matter if the beneficiary intended to enrich themselves through the death; the beneficiary might not even know they were a beneficiary. Since it would be unfair to allow them to benefit from their wrongdoing, the insurer is entitled by law not to perform its obligations under the contract.
Amit and Mary example: Amit purchased life insurance on Mary's life. After a bitter argument, Amit pushed Mary off a cliff. Amit was found guilty of first-degree murder and the insurer was entitled not to pay any benefits — such payments would be contrary to public order.

🏥 Accident and sickness claims

AD&D claims: for accidental injury that causes loss of use of limbs, the payment usually goes to the policyholder (who is usually also the insured person). For loss of life due to accident, the payment usually goes to a named beneficiary or, if none, to the policyholder or the policyholder's estate. Similar to life insurance, the insurer will provide the insured with the necessary forms. Detailed medical evidence from a physician may be necessary.
Disability claims: the claimant must satisfy the insurer that they meet the definition of disability set out in the policy. The disability may be caused by accident or illness. Disability insurance is NOT unemployment insurance — being unable to find a job is not a relevant factor under a disability insurance policy.
Disability documentation sequence: a period of total disability may be required before partial disability benefits are available; the waiting period must elapse before benefits are payable; proof the insured person can no longer perform the duties or activities of their occupation is required; periodic assessment of whether the claimant is still disabled; and assessment of whether they remain permanently, totally, or partially disabled. Financial evidence may be required to support a claim for lost income.
Medical examinations: claims are based on medical evidence from a family doctor or specialist. The insurer may request additional medical evidence or give the claimant the opportunity to consult other specialists (paid by the insurer), since claimants are expected to reasonably assist in their rehabilitation.
Critical illness (CI) claims: a licensed physician specializing in the determined illness must diagnose the insured with the covered illness. A lump-sum benefit payment will be made to the insured 30 days after the claim has been approved. Once the claim is paid, the critical illness insurance policy ceases. If the insured dies for a reason not covered by the CI policy, the premiums paid may be refunded to the named beneficiary.

💰 Segregated funds, annuities and pension claims

Annuity death benefits: when the life insured under an annuity contract dies, the beneficiary or estate is usually entitled to a death benefit. However, this is NOT the case when the annuity contract was an immediate life annuity that has begun paying benefits with no guarantee period, or when the guarantee period has already passed.
Guarantee period annuities: if the life insured dies before the insurer has made a stipulated number of payments, the insurer remains obligated to continue payments. The annuity grantee may receive the remaining payments, or in some cases may agree with the insurer to terminate the contract early and accept a commuted amount in settlement.
Locked-in pension amounts: amounts accumulated in a pension plan may only be transferred to: a LIRA; another pension plan; for the purchase of an immediate life annuity or a deferred life annuity; or a LIF. These four options are the only permitted transfers for locked-in pension amounts.
Death before retirement (key exam concept): when a person receives a pension under a pension plan and their spouse is also eligible under the same plan, if the person dies, the benefits will become payable to the spouse — EVEN IF THE SPOUSE WAS NOT THE DESIGNATED BENEFICIARY. The spouse may be able to roll it into their own locked-in plan. If there were no eligible spouse, the pension benefit becomes payable to the named beneficiary. If there is no named beneficiary, the pension amount is payable to the estate of the pensioner.
Death after retirement: if the pensioner had an eligible spouse, the spouse may be entitled to a survivor's death benefit — usually a reduced amount of the pension that was paid to the pensioner. If there is no eligible spouse, a commuted lump-sum representing the remaining payments in any applicable guarantee period will be payable to the named beneficiary. If there is no named beneficiary, it would be paid to the estate of the pensioner.
