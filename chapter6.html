Chapter 6 - Segregated Fund Contract

This lesson covers everything in depth - the IVIC contract itself, application requirements, FINTRAC and client identification, ongoing service (switches, resets, annual reviews), and the full claims process. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

📄 The segregated fund contract (IVIC)

A segregated fund contract is also called an individual variable insurance contract (IVIC). It spells out conditions for buying, terminating, withdrawing, and receiving benefits. The insurer commits to: guarantees and benefits, valuation methods, premium allocation to guarantees, when value is determined, fee structures, and the right to make contract changes.

Right of rescission: the contract owner can cancel in writing within 2 business days after receiving confirmation of purchase. They receive the lesser of the amount invested or fund value on the next valuation day, plus a refund of fees. The owner is deemed to receive a mailed confirmation 5 business days after mailing - so the rescission clock effectively starts from that deemed date.

✅ The full contract-to-purchase process

Step 1: Complete KYC. Step 2: Make recommendation (reason-why letter). Step 3: Deliver information folder and Fund Facts. Step 4: Complete application. Step 5: Submit to insurer. Step 6: Contract issued (effective date = valuation date following first deposit, once all criteria are met). Step 7: Deliver contract to client (in person or registered mail). Step 8: Confirm right of rescission window. Step 9: Post-sale follow-up. Step 10: Ongoing monitoring and annual review.

Important nuance: delivering a sample contract does NOT constitute purchase - only the actual effective date does.

📝 The application form

Completed jointly by investor and agent (can be done virtually). If language barriers prevent full understanding, the application must wait until a translator or further explanation resolves this. An incomplete application will be REJECTED by the insurer.

POA exception: a person acting under POA for property may sign on the owner's behalf - but POA wording varies, and the attorney may NOT have rights to name the beneficiary. Seek legal opinion given provincial differences. If the agent is unsure the client understands the contract, he must seek advice from his supervisor before proceeding.

📜 The reason-why letter

One of the pillars of insurance sales suitability. Prepared by the agent and provided to the client. It: confirms facts, verifies client instructions, identifies needs the product meets (and leaves uninsured), documents why the sale is suitable, and substantiates the client's understanding. NOT optional - both agent and client must keep a copy.

Spousal RRSP: the RECIPIENT spouse is the owner and annuitant (not the contributing spouse), and only the recipient spouse can name the beneficiary - regardless of who actually funded the plan.

👥 Naming the annuitant and beneficiary

Once named, the annuitant CANNOT be changed (though a contingent annuitant may be named in non-registered contracts to replace him if he dies, if the contract allows). Multiple beneficiaries require a specified percentage allotted to each. Contingent beneficiaries are always good practice - they protect against simultaneous death AND against being left without a valid beneficiary if the primary dies before the owner becomes incompetent to name a replacement.

Irrevocable beneficiaries - use caution: naming one limits the owner's rights, including potentially blocking withdrawals without the beneficiary's consent. In some provinces, this is literally impossible if the irrevocable beneficiary is a minor. Rarely advisable to use.

📋 Rider election (GMWB) form

Required when a contract owner elects a GMWB. The form: specifies the GMWB is being elected, provides a payment option (lifetime or to an end-date), requires fund codes that specifically offer the rider (not every fund qualifies), describes minimum deposit requirements, and sets payment choice/frequency. The rider also addresses how excess withdrawals can significantly reduce future expected payments.

🔍 FINTRAC and client identification

FINTRAC = Canada's financial intelligence unit, operating under the PCMLTFA, fighting money laundering and terrorist financing. Agent obligations apply when a client is expected to deposit $10,000+ over the contract's lifetime, or a beneficiary stands to receive $10,000+ - these requirements do NOT apply to registered contracts.

ID methodKey requirement
Photo IDGovernment-issued, physically present, valid/current, unique ID number
Credit fileMust exist 3+ years; name/address/DOB match (Equifax, TransUnion)
Dual processTwo independent sources - one verifies name+address, other verifies name+DOB or name+financial account
Health card warning: Ontario, Manitoba, Nova Scotia, and PEI prohibit using provincial health cards for ID. Corporations need separate entity-level ID (certificate of corporate status, names of directors) beyond any individual's personal ID.

🚩 Large cash transactions and PEPs

Large cash transaction: a single cash deposit of $10,000+, or multiple cash deposits totaling $10,000+ within 24 hours. Specifically CASH (notes/coins) - not cheques, transfers, or drafts. Many companies prohibit cash transactions outright.

PEFP: held a government/judicial position in a foreign country. Domestic PEP: holds (or held in the last 5 years) a specific federal/provincial/municipal government office. HIO: head/CEO of an international organization set up by multiple governments. Identifying any of these (or their family/close associates) triggers enhanced obligations: record-keeping, source-of-funds verification, senior management approval.

Suspicion thresholds (low to high): simple suspicion (hunch) → reasonable grounds to suspect (probability) → reasonable grounds to believe (probability supported by evidence). NO dollar threshold applies to suspicious transaction reporting - report regardless of amount if suspicion is genuine.

💵 The initial deposit and PADs

Deposit accompanies the application: cheque, transfer, or first pre-authorized deposit (PAD) date. PADs specify frequency, first withdrawal date, ongoing date, banking info, and a void cheque/bank confirmation.

Locked-in transfers: in some jurisdictions, spousal consent is required, or the spouse signs a waiver form (issued by the pension jurisdiction) to release their rights. RRSP/LIRA contracts accept deposits until the end of the year the owner turns 71.

📦 Document delivery: information folder & Fund Facts

Contract must be delivered in person or registered mail. Information folder provides brief, plain disclosure of all material facts about the insurer's IVICs, leading with a summary of key facts. One folder typically bundles Fund Facts for ALL the insurer's funds.

Fund Facts: prepared as of December 31 of the preceding year, presented consistently across all funds for easy comparison, written in plain English. Agent must review Fund Facts with the client before accepting the application - not just hand it over.

📞 Customer instructions

Required for: finalizing purchase, switching funds, reset, withdrawal, contract termination. Confirmation can be electronic or written. Verbal instructions ARE binding when given - but the agent must keep a record of date, time, and method (phone, in person, etc.) to protect against later disputes.

🔄 Fund switches in depth

A switch sells units in one fund to buy units in another. Scheduled switches: automatic, regular (e.g. annual), no fee, no client request needed - though the insurer can cancel/redirect with advance notice. Unscheduled switches: client-requested anytime, subject to minimums; insurers offer a number of free switches per year, beyond which a fee may apply.

Short-term trading fee: switching within 90 days of purchase may trigger a fee (e.g. 2%) or be disallowed - check Fund Facts. A switch CAN trigger capital gains/losses in non-registered contracts based on ACB, and can also act as a reset, changing both maturity date and guarantees.

🔁 Resets in depth

Like switches, resets can be scheduled (automatic) or unscheduled (client-initiated). Not all contracts/funds allow reset. A reset extends the maturity date (e.g. new 10-year term from reset date) AND increases guarantees based on new market value.

Age limits on reset: insurers often cap the age at which reset is allowed (e.g. 80), since resetting near that age would push maturity into a high-mortality period (e.g. age 90), creating significant insurer exposure to fund the death benefit top-up. Unscheduled resets are also capped on frequency per year - don't try to time every market high; steady modest gains are the realistic expectation.

👔 Beneficiary and contract changes

Revocable beneficiaries can be changed anytime by written request or a change-of-beneficiary form. Irrevocable beneficiaries can only be changed with the (competent adult) beneficiary's consent. Changes are effective on the date of SIGNING, but the insurer won't honour them until written notice is RECEIVED - deliver promptly.

Naming the estate as beneficiary: causes the death benefit to pass through probate, losing the bypass advantage that naming an individual provides.

🔁 Contract renewal

At maturity, the contract owner can take cash, buy an annuity, or (if permitted) renew for another period. Renewal starts a NEW guarantee period; the new maturity date depends on the annuitant's age at renewal. The initial contribution to the renewal contract = total market value OR the guarantee, whichever is higher.

⚖️ Claims process and types

Claims can be made by: the contract owner (withdrawal/surrender/maturity), a beneficiary/estate trustee (at death), or a POA holder (withdrawal/surrender/maturity). The agent should NOT attempt to calculate the exact payout - he may be unaware of enhancements (resets) or reductions (withdrawals) affecting the final figure.

Claim typeKey requirement
Maturity/death claim% of deposits, adjusted up by resets, down by withdrawals; insurer tops up if guarantee exceeds market value

RRIF/LIF withdrawal minimums: RRIF requires the standard minimum. LIF/RLIF requires at least the RRIF minimum and no more than the LIF maximum.

📊 Factors affecting the final claim amount

Amount received at maturity/death can exceed the guarantee if market value is higher. But withdrawals during the term proportionally reduce the guarantee base (the % stays the same, the dollar base shrinks). Reset timing also affects the maturity date calculation.

Allocations and ACB: investment income/capital gains generated within the fund stay in the fund (reflected in unit value) and are allocated to investors annually, adjusting the adjusted cost base (ACB) for non-registered contracts - meaning this income is NOT taxed again upon eventual withdrawal.
