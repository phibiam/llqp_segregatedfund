Chapter 8 - Group Retirement Plans

This lesson covers everything in depth - group plan structure, sponsor/administrator roles, all plan types (DBPP, DCPP, GRRSP, DPSP, group LIF/RRIF/TFSA, PRPP), group investment options, the recommendation process, pension credits, plan documents, transfer options, agent service requirements, and the claims process. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

🏢 Group plan structure

In the group context, the agent's CLIENT is the sponsor or administrator - not the individual members. A group plan is comprised of four parties: the sponsor, the administrator, the members, and the insurer. The sponsor registers an RPP with CRA and provincial authorities - or, for federally regulated industries like banking, the plan may be federally regulated instead.

Why CRA registration matters: RPP contributions reduce an individual's available RRSP contribution room, so registration helps track the annual maximum isn't exceeded.

👔 Role of the plan sponsor

The sponsor designs the plan within its financial limitations, sets the benefit structure, and has authority to establish, amend, or terminate the plan. The administrator and agent guide decisions, but final responsibility rests with the sponsor.

Contributory vs non-contributory: contributory = both sponsor AND member contribute (DBPP, DCPP, GRRSP, PRPP can all be contributory). Non-contributory = sponsor alone contributes (DPSP is always non-contributory).

🤝 Matching formulas and vesting

A matching formula bases the sponsor's contribution on the member's own contribution - 100% (dollar-for-dollar) or another percentage. It can be structured to increase over time, reward valuable employees differently, or apply only after a waiting period - all designed to reduce turnover and build loyalty. Matching can be changed or stopped anytime UNLESS locked in by an employment contract or collective agreement.

📋 Plan administrator role and fiduciary duty

The sponsor appoints the administrator - often the sponsor itself, sometimes HR, accounting, or a specialized third party. In Québec, plans with more than 25 members must be administered by a pension committee.

Fiduciary duty: when the employer acts as administrator, it must act honestly, in good faith, and in members' best interests - using the care, diligence, and skill a cautious person would exercise with another's property. Administrator responsibilities include managing the plan, establishing a written investment policy statement, investing assets per that policy, distributing plan documents, enrolling/withdrawing members, and reporting to the sponsor.

📊 Pension statements and member rights

The administrator provides regular pension statements covering plan type, deposits, accumulated earnings, account balance, investment allocation, net return, years of service, projected pension, retirement dates, and beneficiary details.

Leaving the group: when a member leaves (employment change or retirement), the administrator must provide a written statement of rights within a specified period - covering total funds, pension benefit details, transfer/withdrawal options, and the decision deadline.

🎓 Capital accumulation plans (CAP) and guidelines

When a member chooses among 2+ investment options, the plan is a CAP. DCPPs, PRPPs, GRRSPs, TFSAs, and DPSPs are ALL CAPs. DBPPs are NOT, since DBPP members make no investment decisions.

CAP guidelines describe rights/responsibilities of sponsor, service providers, and members - standards ensuring members get the information and assistance needed for suitable investment decisions. They supplement (not replace) legal requirements.

⚖️ Single employer plans vs MEPPs

A single employer plan covers one employer's staff. A multi-employer pension plan (MEPP) covers employees of two or more UNAFFILIATED employers - most common in unionized environments, with fixed contributions set by collective bargaining. This chapter (and PassLLQP coverage) focuses on single-employer plans only.

Important: group plans supplement personal savings - they don't replace the need to save outside the group plan too.

🏛️ DBPP - Defined Benefit Pension Plan

The "gold standard" - pays a KNOWN sum at retirement, generally based on income and years of service. The sponsor is fully responsible for adequate funding; if contributions or returns fall short, the sponsor must make up the shortfall (unless insolvent, in which case pensioners may receive less than promised).

Key facts: members make NO investment choices (sponsor/administrator decides). A pension surplus can trigger taxation or a regulatory/court requirement to enhance benefits. DBPPs can be terminated, converted to a DCPP, or combined as a hybrid plan (required DBPP contributions plus voluntary DCPP top-ups). Income is normally paid as joint and last survivor for the member and spouse.

💼 DCPP - Defined Contribution Pension Plan

Also called a money purchase plan (MPP). No set pension is promised - the eventual pension depends on contributions made, duration, investment choice, and performance. This creates far less financial pressure on the sponsor than a DBPP.

Default option: when a member doesn't choose, almost half of DCPPs default to a target date fund. In ALBERTA specifically, the default is a balanced fund or age-based portfolio instead. The administrator must educate members per CAP guideline obligations - online tools, websites, apps, newsletters, seminars.

💰 Group RRIF and group TFSA

Group RRIF: accepts transfers from GRRSP/DPSP (which are NOT locked-in, unlike DCPP funds); requires minimum annual withdrawal but has NO maximum; must be set up by end of year member turns 71.

Group TFSA: no deductions on contribution, no tax on withdrawal, no tax deferral needed. Contribution room is SHARED across all TFSAs (group + individual combined = one limit). Uniquely flexible: tax-free withdrawals anytime since not locked-in.

🌐 PRPP and group investment options

PRPP (Pooled Registered Pension Plan) targets those without employer pensions or the self-employed; Québec's equivalent is the VRSP. Contributions are locked-in, cashable only for disability, loss of Canadian residency, or a balance under 20% of YMPE. Otherwise funds transfer to a LIRA, LIF, another PRPP, or buy an annuity.

Group segregated funds: available to all plan types EXCEPT DBPP (no member investment choice there). No sales charges, no switch fees, generally lower MER than individual - but NO maturity or death benefit guarantee. Group annuities: a pension buy-in lets a sponsor transfer DBPP funding risk to an insurer with guaranteed payout rates; benefits to retirees are unaffected by the change in provider.

📝 The recommendation process and agent disclosure

The agent must understand member demographics, skills, education, and turnover/loyalty patterns - and never lose sight of group members even though negotiating with the sponsor.

Disclosure obligations: inform the sponsor which insurers the agent places business with (focus on the most regular ones, not an exhaustive list); disclose any insurer loan or commission advance to the agent (a conflict of interest); provide basic compensation structure information (commission/fee/bonus/travel incentive), with SPECIFIC DOLLAR AMOUNTS required if the sponsor asks; obtain written sponsor approval for any post-sale compensation increase. In Québec, exclusive-contract agents must disclose that exclusivity.

🔢 The qualifying factor

Plan design specifies when a member qualifies for a full pension - a set age (e.g. 65), required years of service, or a combination called the qualifying factor (age + years of service). Commonly known by its number, like the "85 factor" (could be 75, 90, or any other number).

Worked example: an employee aged 55 with 30 years of service meets an 85 factor (55+30=85) and could retire with a full pension at 55. A plan may also impose a separate minimum age requirement on top of the factor. If the full factor isn't met, a partial pension may apply per the plan document.

🧮 Pension credits, PA, PSPA, and PAR

Pension credits are created every year EXCEPT in GRRSPs, PRPPs, and group TFSAs. Credits for DBPP/DCPP/DPSP determine the member's pension adjustment (PA), which reduces next year's RRSP room.

Formula: RRSP limit = (prior year's earned income × max % to annual cap) − PA. PAR (pension adjustment reversal): if too much PA was taken (e.g. due to mid-year retirement/termination), the PA is recalculated - the difference is the PAR, restoring RRSP room. PSPA (past service pension adjustment): a retroactive benefit increase (extra credited years, amended formula) creates a PSPA that reduces RRSP room; the administrator must calculate and report it.

📄 Group plan documents and member forms

The administrator completes the group application; once accepted, the contract names the sponsor/administrator as owner. Once approved, members apply individually as they become qualified (after any waiting period).

Member forms: membership enrolment (yields a certificate of membership with ID number), beneficiary naming/change, waiver of joint-and-survivor pension or preretirement death benefit, leave of absence (avoids a new waiting period on return), and notification of intended retirement (filed months ahead).

🛎️ Agent service requirements

The agent seeks regular sponsor input to retain and build the relationship. A previously declined recommendation may become relevant again - the original timing may have been inopportune, and reasons for inaction can change.

Annual review: reinforces plan value and surfaces changing objectives; agent must stay current on trends like PRPP introduction. Change in circumstances: report workforce changes, sponsor financial shifts, and administrator changes to the insurer. Plan wind-up triggers: business sale to a non-pension buyer, downsizing/closure, most members no longer employed, or sponsor bankruptcy/insolvency - plan value is held in TRUST and cannot be accessed by the sponsor even in bankruptcy.

⚰️ Claims by group members

A claim requires the group name and certificate/ID number. The claim form must be completed by the member; if he dies, the spouse is called upon for information - this needs efficient handling given the spouse's potential urgent need for the proceeds.

Exceptional circumstances unlocking: locked-in funds can sometimes be accessed early/in larger amounts for shortened life expectancy, proven financial hardship, non-residency, a small account balance, or partial LIF/RLIF withdrawal. These exceptions VARY by province/territory and by provincial vs federal regulation - always verify the specific governing jurisdiction before assuming an unlocking rule applies.
