Chapter 2 — Segregated Funds

Segregated funds are one of the most heavily tested topics on the LLQP. This lesson covers everything in depth — the contract parties, every guarantee mechanism, all fund types, sales charges, and taxation. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

🏦 What is a segregated fund?

A segregated fund is an insurance contract — not a mutual fund. It is issued by a life insurance company and lets clients invest in a pool of assets while receiving insurance guarantees. Only LLQP-licensed advisors can sell them.

The assets are kept "segregated" (separate) from the insurance company's general assets — so if the insurer goes bankrupt, your client's money is protected.

Key difference from mutual funds: Seg funds offer maturity guarantees, death benefit guarantees, potential creditor protection, and bypass of probate. Mutual funds offer none of these. This guarantee package is why seg fund MERs run higher — investors are paying for insurance, not just fund management.

👥 The three parties to a segregated fund contract

Every segregated fund contract involves three roles, and understanding who plays each role is essential for understanding how the guarantees actually work.

PartyRole
Policy ownerDeposits the principal, makes investment decisions, can withdraw funds, and receives the maturity guarantee
Annuitant (measuring life)The person whose death triggers the death benefit guarantee
BeneficiaryNamed by the policy owner; receives the death benefit when the annuitant dies
Registered plan rule: If the contract is inside an RRSP or RRIF, the policy owner MUST also be the annuitant. In a non-registered (taxable) contract, the policy owner can name anyone as annuitant.

🛡️ The two core guarantees

Maturity guarantee (75% or 100%): At the contract maturity date, the client receives the greater of the market value OR the guaranteed percentage of deposits. Example: $100,000 deposited with 75% guarantee = minimum $75,000 at maturity regardless of market performance.

Death benefit guarantee (75% or 100%): If the annuitant dies during the contract, the beneficiary receives the greater of market value OR the guaranteed percentage of deposits.

Top-up: If market value is below the guarantee, the insurer pays the difference from its own reserves. This is called a "top-up." Example: Market value = $60,000, guarantee = $75,000 → insurer pays $15,000 top-up. If the contract is extended rather than cashed out at maturity, the top-up is attributed to the contract rather than paid in cash.

The two guarantees do NOT have to match — a contract could offer 75%/100% (maturity/death benefit), and some insurers even let clients reset each guarantee independently.

⚠️ When the guarantee does NOT apply

The maturity guarantee applies only on the actual maturity date. If a client surrenders or makes a partial withdrawal before maturity, they receive only the current market value (less any applicable fees) — never the guarantee. This is one of the most heavily tested concepts in this chapter.

Withdrawals reduce the guarantee proportionally. The guarantee percentage itself (75% or 100%) never changes, but it's applied to a smaller remaining deposit base after a withdrawal. A $100,000 deposit with a 75% guarantee ($75,000) reduced by a 20% withdrawal leaves a new guarantee base of $80,000 × 75% = $60,000.

📅 Deposit-date vs. contract-date guarantees

When an investor makes a series of deposits over time (rather than one lump sum), the maturity guarantee can be structured two ways.

Deposit-date based: each individual deposit has its own maturity guarantee and its own term-to-maturity, starting from that deposit's own date. A client depositing annually over several years will have a staggered series of maturity dates.

Contract-date based: all deposits share a single guarantee tied to the original contract date.

Example: Sandra has a deposit-date-based contract with a 15-year term. She deposits $2,000 initially and $2,000 in each of the next two years. Her first deposit's guarantee ($1,500) matures 15 years from year one; her second deposit's guarantee matures 16 years from year one; her third matures 17 years from year one.

🔄 Reset — locking in gains

If the fund grows in value, the client can "reset" their guarantee to the new higher value. This locks in market gains for guarantee purposes — both the policy owner (via the maturity guarantee) and the beneficiary (via the death benefit guarantee) benefit from a reset.

Important trade-off: Resetting also restarts the maturity date clock when the policy owner initiates the reset manually. A reset on a 10-year contract restarts the 10 years from the reset date. Advisors must disclose this before processing a reset. Some insurers offer automatic reset, which may not necessarily extend the maturity date, depending on the contract.

Not every contract offers reset, and some only allow a limited number of resets. There may also be a fee associated with locking in a higher guarantee.

💵 Guaranteed income: GMWB and GLWB

Some segregated fund contracts allow the client to draw a guaranteed stream of income before maturity, addressing longevity risk — the risk of outliving one's savings.

FeatureGMWBGLWB
Full nameGuaranteed Minimum Withdrawal BenefitGuaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal Benefit
Payment durationSet period of timeFor as long as the annuitant is alive

Both plans have three phases. In the savings phase, the investor accumulates value and may earn an annual credit (e.g. 5% of the initial deposit) in any year no withdrawal is made. In the payout phase, regular withdrawals begin based on the guaranteed minimum balance — the annual income is insulated from poor market performance, even as the underlying contract value declines. In the benefits phase, the contract value has reached zero but the insurer continues paying the guaranteed income.

Lifetime payouts are lower than fixed-period payouts of the same value, because the contract value must be stretched over a potentially longer, unknown period — similar to how a life annuity pays less than a term-certain annuity. Withdrawing more than the guaranteed amount triggers fees and reduces the base for future guaranteed income.

📊 Tracking value: units, NAVPU, and ACB

Every segregated fund deposit buys a number of units, calculated as deposit ÷ unit value. The unit value itself is called the NAVPU (Net Asset Value Per Unit), recalculated on each valuation day.

NAVPU = (Total assets − Liabilities) ÷ Number of units outstanding

The number of units an investor holds doesn't change with market performance — only the NAVPU changes. Units only change when the investor makes an additional deposit (buying more units) or a withdrawal (selling units).

In a non-registered contract, interest, dividends, and capital gains earned in the fund are allocated to the investor each year for tax purposes, even though no cash is paid out — these allocations also increase the investor's adjusted cost base (ACB), which prevents that same income from being taxed twice when units are eventually sold or the contract is surrendered.

👔 Professional management and diversification

Insurance companies hire professional fund managers who are compensated through the MER. The average investor lacks the time and expertise to compete with a trained fund manager, so this delegation of decision-making is a core selling point — though it never eliminates the advisor's own responsibility to ensure the fund continues meeting the client's objectives over time.

Segregated funds are highly diversified within their category — an equity fund, for example, holds shares of many companies. Even in the unlikely scenario that all underlying assets lost their value, the investor is still protected up to the maturity and death benefit guarantee.

💰 Tax benefits of segregated funds

The insurer tracks the ACB on behalf of the investor — virtually eliminating the risk of reporting errors that can occur when a mutual fund investor must self-track this calculation.

Non-registered seg fund earnings flow through to the investor on a flow-through basis: interest is reported as interest, dividends as dividends, and capital gains/losses as such — each taxed according to its own rules. Capital losses can offset gains in the same year, be carried back 3 years, or carried forward indefinitely.

Probate bypass is a major (non-tax) advantage: death benefit proceeds paid to a named beneficiary (other than the estate) bypass probate entirely, saving fees and avoiding the delays of estate settlement.

🔀 Switching between funds

Investors can switch their contract value from one fund to another as objectives or risk tolerance change. Before a switch, the agent must provide the same disclosure given at initial purchase, including the most recent Fund Facts document for the new fund.

Switching between funds with the same sales charge option (e.g. both front-end load) typically incurs no fee. Switching between funds with dissimilar loads (e.g. no-load to front-end load) may trigger a fee. Frequent or very short-term switches may also trigger separate trading fees. Contract guarantees themselves are not affected by a fund switch, but in a non-registered contract, a switch can trigger a taxable disposition — a capital gain or loss — even without any cash being withdrawn.

💸 Withdrawals (redemption)

Investors can withdraw at any time by redeeming units at their market value on the relevant valuation day, with proceeds typically received within days. A withdrawal reduces both the contract's remaining units and the guarantee base proportionally, and may trigger taxable income or capital gains that must be reported.

⚖️ Probate, estate planning, and creditor protection

Probate fees are charged by the province against the value of a deceased's estate before assets can be distributed — except in Quebec and Manitoba, which don't charge probate fees at all.

A named beneficiary (other than the estate) on a segregated fund contract receives the proceeds directly, bypassing probate, its fees, delays, and public disclosure. If the estate IS named as beneficiary, all of these protections are lost — proceeds become part of the estate, subject to probate and creditor claims.

Creditor protection may also apply when a "preferred class" beneficiary is named: spouse, parent, child, or grandchild. This protection is strongest with an irrevocable designation. However, this protection is not absolute — the contract must be acquired for genuine investment purposes, not solely to evade creditors, and clients should seek independent legal advice.

🏢 Assuris protection

Assuris protects policyholders if the life insurance company issuing the seg fund becomes insolvent, facilitating a quick transfer of policies to a financially capable insurer.

Assuris coverage formula: the greater of $100,000 or 90% of the guaranteed amount. Assuris protection is only relevant when the guarantee exceeds market value at the time of insolvency — if market value is already higher than the guarantee, there's no shortfall for Assuris to cover.

Worked example: Mark deposited $150,000 with a 75% guarantee ($112,500). His insurer becomes insolvent when market value is $98,000. Assuris pays the greater of $100,000 or 90% × $112,500 ($101,250) — so Mark receives $101,250.

✅ Absence of medical underwriting & right of rescission

Most segregated fund contracts require no medical underwriting. This means an older or unhealthy applicant's health doesn't affect their coverage or guarantee — though each contract still sets a maximum age for making deposits.

The right of rescission lets an investor cancel a new contract or deposit in writing, typically within 2 business days of receiving the confirmation statement (the investor is deemed to have received it 5 business days after mailing). On rescission, the investor receives the lesser of the premium paid or the unit value on the relevant valuation date.

💳 Sales charge (load) options

OptionHow it works
Front-end load (FEL)Fee charged at deposit, negotiable (typically 0%–5%), deducted from the amount invested
Fee for serviceClient and agent negotiate a recurring fee; insurer pays no commission
No-load / FEL ZeroNo upfront fee; agent earns an ongoing trailing commission instead
Advisor chargebackAgent gets paid upfront; must repay the insurer if client withdraws early — creates a conflict-of-interest risk
Deferred sales charge (DSC)No upfront fee; client pays a declining redemption fee (up to 7%) if they withdraw within a set period (up to 7 years) — being phased out across Canada
A trailing commission is an ongoing fee built into the MER, paid to the agent for as long as the client holds the fund — it compensates for continued advice and service, not just the initial sale.

📉 Limitations of segregated fund contracts

Even with their guarantees, segregated funds have real limitations. The most fundamental is risk to capital: a 75% guarantee still leaves 25% of deposits unprotected, and that protection only applies at maturity or death — not on early withdrawal.

The MER on a segregated fund is typically higher than a comparable mutual fund, since part of it funds the reserve backing the guarantees (sometimes called the "insurance charge"). Withdrawals and surrenders may also trigger penalties in the contract's early months, and contracts impose age restrictions — for example, an RRSP-based seg fund follows standard RRSP rules, with deposits allowed only until December 31 of the year the owner turns 71.

🏛️ Taxation of segregated funds

Taxation depends on two things: whether the contract is taxable (non-registered or registered like an RRSP/RRIF) or non-taxable (TFSA), and the form of investment income earned.

ScenarioTax treatment
Deposits into a non-registered plan or TFSANot tax-deductible
Deposits into most registered plans (e.g. RRSP)Tax-deductible (except RESP, RDSP)
Non-registered allocations (interest, dividends, capital gains)Reported annually, even if not withdrawn — taxed per income type
Registered contract allocationsNot reported annually — tax-deferred until withdrawal
RRSP/RRIF withdrawalFully taxable as ordinary income, subject to withholding tax (except the RRIF minimum required withdrawal)
TFSA withdrawalCompletely tax-free
Top-up taxation: if the contract is non-registered, a maturity or death benefit top-up is taxed as a capital gain (50% inclusion). If registered, the top-up is taxed as income. A surviving spouse named as beneficiary on a registered contract can defer this tax by rolling proceeds into their own RRSP or RRIF. Top-ups for TFSA guarantees are never taxable.

🤝 Fair Treatment of Customers (FTC)

FTC requires agents to act ethically and in good faith, recommending products based on client needs — not commission or incentives. In practice, this means: selling products that pay due regard to client interests; providing clear, accurate, non-misleading information before, during, and after the sale; minimizing unsuitable sales; ensuring high-quality advice; handling complaints fairly and promptly; and protecting client privacy.

When two products are equally suitable for a client but pay different compensation, the agent must disclose that conflict of interest — recommending the higher-paying option without disclosure is a clear FTC violation, regardless of whether the product was technically suitable.
