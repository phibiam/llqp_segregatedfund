Chapter 1 — Legal Framework Governing Life Insurance

Insurance agents play an essential role in the insurance market. This chapter provides an overview of the legal framework applicable to insurance — covering sources of Canadian law, the structure of the insurance industry, key legal principles affecting clients, public insurance programs, and other legislation agents must know.

⚖️ Sources of Canadian insurance law

Three sources of law: the Canadian Constitution; Common Law; and legislation and regulation. The Constitution takes precedence over all other sources of law — federal and provincial legislation must comply with it.
Constitution Act, 1867: regulates the division of legislative powers between federal and provincial governments. Insurance law is considered an exclusive provincial matter under the Constitution.
Constitution Act, 1982: contains the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. The repatriation of the Constitution from the United Kingdom occurred with its passing on April 17, 1982.
Common Law: unwritten law developed by courts based on the decisions of judges (case law). Applies everywhere in Canada except Québec (which uses the Civil Code of Québec). Principles applicable to contracts, torts, property, trusts, and agency are mostly governed by Common Law in Canada.
Insurance legislation: insurance law has been mostly enacted through legislation passed by provincial and territorial legislatures. While each province has its own legislation, they are all based on a model statute created in the 1920s in cooperation with the Uniform Law Conference — making insurance legislation very similar across Canada.
Applicable law for contracts: the law that applies to the interpretation of an insurance contract (including beneficiary designations) will be the law of the province or territory where the policy was contracted. If a policyholder moves to another province after the policy is issued, that province's laws will apply to certain aspects of their estate planning (such as their will).

🏢 The insurance industry structure

Insurance contract defined: an undertaking by one person to indemnify another against loss, or to pay a sum of money upon the happening of a certain event. Includes life insurance. Annuity contracts issued by insurers are considered life insurance contracts under provincial and territorial legislation.
Life insurance: covers the risk of one or more persons dying. Accident and sickness insurance covers loss of health including: disability; critical illness; accidental death; accidental dismemberment; reimbursement of medical fees and drugs; and long-term care needs.
Agency relationship: in law, a relationship of delegated authority is called agency. The person delegating the authority is the principal. In common law provinces, the relationship between the insurance company and a life insurance agent is that of a principal and agent — agents act on behalf of the insurance company.
Independent agents: have distribution contracts with two or more insurers (not of the same financial group) and can sell policies from multiple companies. They generally place their business through managing general agents (MGAs).
Career agents (captive/exclusive agents): represent a single insurer or are bound by contractual stipulations to represent a single MGA or insurance marketing entity. May be an employee of a specific insurance company or a self-employed worker with an exclusivity clause.
Managing General Agent (MGA): facilitates business between agents, their clients, and insurers. Services include: supporting agents in obtaining contracts with insurers; processing and tracking business; providing direct sales support; facilitating information flow between insurer and agent; pooling commission payments; training agents; providing market conduct compliance supports; and developing products or assisting in claims adjusting on behalf of an insurer.
Insurance agency: either a corporation or a partnership that holds a licence from a regulator. Must have at least one licensed insurance agent for the same category of insurance. Any employee of an agency who acts as a life insurance agent must be licensed — including anyone who solicits insurance, obtains applications, negotiates insurance, transmits applications/policies, collects premiums, or develops illustrations for life insurance.
Regulation and licensing: insurance law is an exclusive provincial matter. Provincial and territorial insurance legislation gives designated actuaries (who must belong to the Canadian Institute of Actuaries) extensive powers and responsibilities including reporting on financial soundness. Insurance agents must be licensed under provincial and territorial law. Regulatory bodies vary by province (e.g., FSRA in Ontario, BCFSA/Insurance Council of BC in BC, Alberta Insurance Council in AB).

👤 Key legal principles affecting clients

Legal capacity: anyone seeking to enter into a contract must have legal capacity — the ability to provide valid consent. A person 18 years of age or more is presumed capable of entering into a contract. A person 16 years of age or more is presumed capable of giving or refusing consent for personal care. There is NO recognized maximum age at which a person is presumed incapable — a 100-year-old is still presumed to have legal capacity.
Insurance contracting by minors: provincial insurance legislation says that a person 16 years of age or older has the capacity to contract for insurance on their own behalf (except as a beneficiary receiving death benefits). Parents of minors aged 15 and under have an insurable interest in their children and may apply for a life insurance policy in their name.
Age of majority: age 18 in Alberta, Manitoba, Ontario, PEI, Québec, and Saskatchewan. Age 19 in BC, New Brunswick, Newfoundland and Labrador, Nova Scotia, NWT, Nunavut, and Yukon.
Power of Attorney (PoA): a legal document made by one person appointing another (called the attorney or agent) to make financial and legal decisions on their behalf. A PoA ends if the person who gave it becomes mentally incapable.
Enduring Power of Attorney: continues even if the principal becomes mentally incapable. Must state whether the attorney can act while the person is capable or only while incapable, and that the authority continues despite incapability. The attorney usually has all powers required to deal with property but NOT to make a will. Most insurers will refuse a beneficiary designation made by an attorney.
Agents and powers of attorney: insurance agents should obtain a certified true copy of any power of attorney from a notary public and have it reviewed by the insurance company before acting on it. Agents should decline to act as a client's power of attorney — this is not covered by professional errors and omissions insurance and could create conflicts of interest.
Guardianship: unlike enduring powers of attorney, guardianship only comes into effect after a person has been declared incapable. A court-appointed guardian may accept death benefit payments on behalf of a minor, or complete an insurance application on behalf of a young minor. If there is no person capable of giving a valid discharge for insurance money payable to a minor, the insurer shall pay the money into court within 30 days.
Marriage vs. common law: property rights of married spouses usually differ significantly from those of common law spouses when their relationship breaks down. Married status = presumed division of family property; common law status = no presumed division in most provinces. Married status can only be terminated by death, annulment, or a legally valid divorce. Agents should advise clients to seek competent legal advice on the effects of marriage vs. common law status on their financial and legal arrangements.
Divorce and separation: in the event of divorce or separation, the value of the policy may become subject to the valuation and division of property. Insurance proceeds received from a third party may receive preferential treatment similar to inheritances and gifts. Separation of married partners has no effect on a will — clients who wish to disinherit a former spouse must change their will, beneficiary designations, and sign a separation agreement. Divorce does NOT revoke a will (except the provisions referring to the spouse) and does NOT revoke beneficiary designations (except in Québec).
Wills and estates: dying testate = dying with a will; dying intestate = dying without a will. The executor (or estate trustee) manages and distributes the estate according to the will. Property held in joint tenancy, registered plans with named beneficiaries (RRSP, RRIF, TFSA), and life insurance payable to a named beneficiary all pass OUTSIDE the will. A will made before marriage is automatically revoked upon marriage (unless made with the marriage in mind).
Trusts: inter-vivos trusts are established during the settlor's life; testamentary trusts are established upon death. Unlike powers of attorney, trustees have legal title to the property. Once a trust has been created, the trustee is under a positive legal obligation to act. Insurance proceeds can be used to create or enhance a trust fund. Trustees can hold an insurance policy, pay premiums, and collect/handle proceeds in accordance with the trust terms.
Bankruptcy: provincial insurance legislation states that insurance proceeds payable to beneficiaries do NOT form part of the insured's estate and are therefore not subject to control by the estate's creditors. While a designation in favour of a spouse, child, grandchild, or parent of the life insured is in effect, the insurance money AND the rights and interests of the insured in the contract are exempt from execution or seizure. An irrevocable beneficiary designation — even outside the preferred family class — also renders the policy exempt from execution. Asset protection is one reason agents usually advise naming direct beneficiaries rather than the estate. Pension funds, and in some jurisdictions RRSPs and RRIFs, are also generally exempt from seizure.

📋 Contract law and torts

Valid contract requirements: a contract must be created by parties with shared intention of entering into a binding agreement (meeting of the minds). The subject matter must be acceptable under public policy. Contracts against public policy are unenforceable and void.
Tort: an action or omission that causes someone loss or harm for which the wrongdoer is liable. Tort claims are not limited to physical loss — they include injury to reputation (defamation), emotional injury, economic injury, violations of privacy, violations of property, and violations of constitutional rights. A breach of an agent's privacy obligations could lead to a civil action in torts.
Limitation periods: a timeframe during which a court action must be started — once it expires, the right to sue is lost forever. Once someone discovers civil wrongdoing has been done to them, the limitation period clock starts ticking. Agents must be aware of limitation periods in their jurisdiction.

🏛️ Public insurance and retirement regimes

Federally sponsored programs: Canada Pension Plan (CPP); Old Age Security (OAS) and Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS); Employment Insurance (EI). The purpose of CPP, OAS, and GIS is to provide a secure, modest base upon which to build additional retirement income — they are NOT intended to meet all retirement income needs.
CPP: mandatory pension plan funded by contributions (not by government taxes). Employers contribute an equal amount for their employees. Individuals may begin drawing a reduced pension after age 60 or an enhanced pension at age 70. CPP contributors may also draw a disability benefit if they become permanently and totally disabled. There are provisions for a death benefit and survivor benefits for spouses and children.
OAS and GIS: OAS is non-contributory and government-funded through duties and taxes. The benefit amount depends on how long the resident has been in Canada since age 18, spousal status, and pensioner status of the other spouse. OAS pension benefits are taxable income. The GIS is a non-taxable supplement for those already getting the OAS pension.
EI: provides temporary financial assistance to unemployed Canadians who lost their job through no fault of their own. A minimum number of insurable hours are required before becoming eligible. Self-employed individuals are not eligible for EI benefits. Some benefits received may have to be repaid as part of income tax filing obligations.
Provincially sponsored programs: no-fault automobile insurance; workers' compensation; universal health care and drug plan. These provide public coverage in certain circumstances and avoid lawsuits. In BC, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and Québec, the province provides the basic automobile insurance benefits; in other provinces these rules are incorporated into provincial law.
Workers' compensation: compensation and rehabilitation benefits available to workers on a no-fault basis. Funded by mandatory employer contributions through a payroll tax. In exchange for these benefits, workers give up the right to sue their employers.
Universal health care: 13 separate provincial and territorial plans coordinated under the Canada Health Act. Canadian Dental Care Plan (CDCP) began December 2023, covering residents with adjusted family net income below $90,000 who don't have dental insurance. The Pharmacare Act (October 2024) has signed bilateral agreements with BC, Manitoba, PEI, and Yukon, making contraceptives and diabetes medications free at pharmacy.
Coordination of benefits: when spouses are both entitled to private health/drug/dental plan benefits, benefit companies apply coordination of benefits so one plan pays first and the second pays only the unpaid balances. Private coverage is only supposed to address services not covered by a provincial plan.

📜 Other key legislation for agents

Privacy Act and PIPEDA: the Privacy Act governs how the federal government handles personal information. PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act) sets out ground rules for how businesses may collect, use, or disclose personal information in commercial activities. Under PIPEDA, only information that is needed and relevant to conduct a business transaction should be collected. Three provinces have passed laws similar to PIPEDA (Alberta, BC, Québec) that take precedence in those provinces.
Human rights legislation: discrimination is forbidden on the basis of gender, religion, skin colour, national origin, family status, marital status, sexual orientation, and age. However, insurers are permitted to discriminate in providing insurance coverage as long as they can justify it on reasonable and bona fide grounds.
Criminal Code: defines behaviour and actions that constitute a crime, including more serious offences that may result in jail time.
PCMLTFA (Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act): aims to detect individuals and corporations involved in criminal activity and prevent them from getting illegally obtained money into the banking system. Reporting suspicious transactions and suspected possession of terrorist property is the personal responsibility of every insurance agent. Agents face severe personal penalties and even imprisonment if they fail to report. Reports must be made to FINTRAC (Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada).
National Do Not Call List (DNCL): clients may register phone numbers to prevent telemarketers from calling them. Calls to businesses are not prohibited. Agents making telemarketing calls must register and subscribe to the national DNCL. If agents hire telemarketers, those telemarketers must comply with the DNCL.
CASL (Canadian Anti-Spam Legislation): effective July 1, 2014, prohibits sending commercial electronic messages (CEM) without the recipient's consent. The CEM must have prescribed consent and a mechanism to unsubscribe. A request to unsubscribe must be honoured immediately and no later than within 10 business days.
Genetic Non-Discrimination Act: confirmed constitutional by the Supreme Court of Canada. Prohibits anyone from forcing a person to undergo a genetic test as a prerequisite for providing goods or services, entering into a contract, or offering specific contract terms. Also prohibits communication of genetic test results for these purposes.
