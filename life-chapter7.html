Chapter 7 - Taxation of Life Insurance and Tax Strategies

This lesson covers everything in depth - the tax-free death benefit, policy dispositions and gains, the adjusted cost basis (ACB) and how it works for G1/G2/G3 policies, taxation of dividends, full and partial surrenders, policy loans and repayments, exempt vs. non-exempt policies (the MTAR and anti-dump-in rules), replacement policies, absolute assignments (including spousal and child rollovers), death of the policyholder, and key tax strategies: collateral/business loans, annuitizing the CSV, the insured retirement strategy, and charitable giving. Take your time - the calculations build on each other throughout.

💰 The tax-free death benefit

The death benefit from a personally held life insurance policy is EXEMPT from tax - regardless of how long the policy has been in place, how much the policyholder has paid in premiums, or the type of policy (term, permanent, group, mortgage, etc.). The death benefit includes the ENTIRE amount paid out upon death of the life insured, not just the original face amount - so for a UL policy with a level-plus-account-value death benefit, the beneficiary receives both the face amount AND the investment account value, all tax-free.

Worked example - Susan and Andrew: Susan bought a $500,000 whole life policy just months ago, paying only $2,300 in premiums. Andrew dies. Susan receives the full $500,000 tax-free - the size and recency of premiums paid is irrelevant.
Worked example - Carl and Sarah: Carl's $100,000 UL policy (level plus account value) has an account value of $2,993 after $9,250 in premiums. If Carl dies, Sarah receives $102,993 (face amount plus account value) entirely tax-free.
Important caveat: any benefits the policyholder receives WHILE the policy is in force (i.e., before the life insured's death) may have immediate tax consequences, unlike the death benefit itself.

📋 Policy dispositions: actual and deemed

A disposition occurs when the policyholder gives up all ownership rights to the policy by transferring or surrendering it. A deemed disposition occurs when the conditions in the Income Tax Act for transfer of property are met, even though there is no actual sale or gift - the policy is considered to have been transferred for tax purposes even though it has not changed hands.

Common events triggering an actual or deemed disposition: surrendering all or part of the policy, withdrawing cash, taking out a policy loan, a participating policy paying dividends under certain dividend options, the policy becoming non-exempt, absolutely assigning or transferring ownership to another party, and the policyholder dying and ownership transferring to another party.
Proceeds of disposition: any amount the policyholder receives (or is deemed to receive) as a result of a disposition. The tax consequences depend on what those proceeds are and the policy's adjusted cost basis (ACB).

📊 Policy gains: the core formula

When a policyholder disposes of all or a portion of his interest in a life insurance policy, he may realize a policy gain, which is reported as 100% taxable income (not a capital gain - the full amount is added to income).

The formula: Policy gain = Proceeds of disposition minus Adjusted Cost Basis (ACB)
Worked example - Brian: Brian surrenders his whole life policy and receives proceeds of $46,000. The insurer advises his ACB is $20,000. Policy gain = $46,000 minus $20,000 = $26,000, all of which is taxable income.

🧮 Adjusted cost basis (ACB): the concept

The ACB is the cost of the policy for tax purposes, defined in the Income Tax Act. A bigger ACB means a smaller policy gain and a smaller tax liability. The insurer provides the ACB when a taxable disposition is considered or occurs. Some transactions INCREASE the ACB (premiums paid, policy loan repayments, interest paid on a loan that is not deductible); others DECREASE it (net cost of pure insurance, policy loan taken out, dividends paid in cash, certain partial surrenders).

Key insight: a policy gain equals proceeds minus ACB, so understanding what moves the ACB is central to understanding how much tax will be owed when a policy is disposed of.

📅 G1, G2, and G3 policies: the three groups

How a policy's ACB is calculated depends on when the policy was "last acquired" - meaning the latest of: when it was first purchased, when ownership was transferred to the current policyholder, or when coverage was last modified or reinstated.

G1 (grandfathered): acquired BEFORE December 2, 1982, and not transferred or modified since. These policies have significant tax advantages. A G1 policy loses its status and becomes G2 if ownership is transferred or coverage is modified.
G2: acquired after December 1, 1982 but before January 1, 2017, OR previously G1 policies that have lost that status. A G2 policy becomes G3 if it is converted to another type of policy on or after January 1, 2017, or if any coverage requiring medical underwriting is added after January 1, 2017 (such as increasing coverage, adding a term rider, or substituting the life insured).
G3: issued after December 31, 2016, OR previously G2 policies that have lost that status.

🔢 ACB for G1 policies

For G1 (grandfathered) policies, the ACB is simply the cumulative premiums paid to date, minus cumulative dividends paid out in cash. This method generally produces a HIGHER ACB than the G2/G3 methods, meaning a lower policy gain upon disposition. G1 policies are also not subject to exempt testing and not subject to annual accrual taxation, making them the most tax-advantaged group.

🔢 ACB for G2 and G3 policies

For G2 and G3 policies, ACB equals cumulative premiums MINUS the net cost of pure insurance (NCPI). The NCPI is a measure of the pure mortality costs under the policy for a given year, combining the net amount at risk and the mortality risk for the life insured.

How ACB moves over time: in the early years, ACB grows because premiums exceed NCPI. As the life insured ages, NCPI rises to the point where it exceeds premiums, causing the ACB to decline. The ACB can reduce to zero, but NEVER goes negative.
G3 vs. G2 difference: G3 policies use updated standardized mortality tables reflecting higher life expectancies, resulting in a LOWER NCPI in early years than an equivalent G2 policy. This means G3 ACBs grow faster in early years and take longer to reach zero - generally a tax advantage for policyholders who may dispose of the policy early in its life.
ACB adjustment summary - what increases ACB: premiums (including ratings), dividends used for paid-up additions or additional term insurance or to repay a policy loan, interest paid on a policy loan when not deductible elsewhere, and policy loan repayments in excess of any gain already reported. What decreases ACB: NCPI, dividends paid in cash, the amount of a policy loan taken out, a prorated portion of the ACB upon partial surrender, and any policy gain already included in income from a prior loan, withdrawal, or partial surrender.

💵 Taxation of policy dividends

The payment of policy dividends may trigger a deemed disposition. The policy gain is: (policy dividend minus amount used to pay an eligible premium) minus the policy's ACB.

When no tax arises: if dividends are directed to internal transactions (paid-up additions, additional term insurance, repaying a policy loan), the proceeds of disposition are zero, so no policy gain and no taxable income results. Premiums increase the ACB; dividends paid in cash reduce it - so most cash dividends end up being treated as a tax-free return of premiums.
G2 caveat: for G2 policies, premiums for ACB purposes exclude amounts paid for accidental death benefits, disability payments, ratings for substandard lives, cost of conversion rights or GIB, or other supplementary benefits. If a G2 or G3 policy includes these benefits and pays cash dividends, a policy gain may arise.

❌ Taxation of a full surrender

A full surrender is when the policyholder cancels the policy entirely with no further rights or obligations for either party - this is a disposition for tax purposes. The proceeds of disposition equal the CSV minus any outstanding policy loans (including interest) and unpaid premiums.

Worked example - Alicia: $200,000 whole life policy, CSV = $24,000, ACB = $10,000, no outstanding loans. Policy gain = $24,000 minus $10,000 = $14,000. At a 35% marginal rate: tax payable = $14,000 x 35% = $4,900. Net cash after tax = $24,000 minus $4,900 = $19,100.

✂️ Taxation of a partial surrender: reducing coverage

A partial surrender occurs when the policyholder reduces the amount of coverage or withdraws from a UL investment account without fully cancelling. For G2 policies, the policy gain is calculated on a PRORATED basis based on the percentage of coverage surrendered.

Worked example - Alicia (partial): reduces coverage from $200,000 to $150,000 - a 25% surrender. CSV portion = $24,000 x 25% = $6,000. Prorated ACB = $10,000 x 25% = $2,500. Policy gain = $6,000 minus $2,500 = $3,500. Tax = $3,500 x 35% = $1,225.
G3 partial surrender: also prorated, but the denominator is the policy's NET cash value (CSV less outstanding policy loans), not just the CSV.
G1 partial surrender: NO prorating. Withdrawals only trigger a policy gain once CUMULATIVE withdrawals exceed the policy's ENTIRE ACB.

💸 Taxation of policy withdrawals from UL investment accounts

For a UL policy, a withdrawal from the investment account even without reducing coverage is also treated as a partial surrender. For G2 policies the prorated ACB is: (amount withdrawn divided by cash value of investment account) multiplied by ACB of policy.

Worked example - Janine: $200,000 UL policy, ACB = $65,000, investment account cash value = $80,000. She withdraws $40,000. Prorated ACB = ($40,000 divided by $80,000) x $65,000 = $32,500. Policy gain = $40,000 minus $32,500 = $7,500. Tax = $7,500 x 35% = $2,625.
G3: same approach but uses NET cash value (CSV less outstanding loans) as the denominator. G1: no prorating - gains only when cumulative withdrawals exceed the full ACB.

🏦 Taxation of policy loans

Taking a policy loan is a deemed disposition. The proceeds of disposition equal the loan amount, minus any portion of the loan applied to pay a premium. For an automatic premium loan (APL), the deemed proceeds are NIL because the entire loan pays premiums.

Loan less than ACB: no policy gain, but the ACB is reduced by the loan amount. Example - Alicia borrows $4,000 against a $10,000 ACB: new ACB = $10,000 minus $4,000 = $6,000, no tax.
Loan greater than ACB: policy gain = loan amount minus ACB. The ACB drops to zero. Example - Alicia borrows $19,000 against a $10,000 ACB: policy gain = $19,000 minus $10,000 = $9,000. Tax = $9,000 x 35% = $3,150. ACB is now $0.

🔄 Repaying a policy loan and interest deductibility

When a policyholder repays a policy loan, he can DEDUCT the repayment from taxable income - but only up to the amount of the policy gain he had to report when he originally took out the loan. Any repayment above that amount INCREASES the policy's ACB.

Worked example - Alicia repays: she repays $12,000 of her $19,000 policy loan (which had triggered a $9,000 gain). Tax deduction = $9,000 (the previously reported gain). ACB increase = $12,000 minus $9,000 = $3,000. New ACB = $3,000.
Policy loan interest: if the loan proceeds are invested to earn property or business income (dividends, rent, interest - but NOT capital gains only), the interest is tax-deductible. If the loan is for personal use or solely to realize capital gains, interest is NOT deductible. Non-deductible interest INCREASES the policy's ACB.

🛡️ Exempt vs. non-exempt policies: the big picture

An exempt policy allows the investment account to grow TAX-DEFERRED or TAX-FREE within the policy. All G1 policies are ALWAYS exempt. G2 and G3 policies must pass an "exempt test" designed to ensure the policy is primarily for insurance purposes, not as an investment vehicle disguised as insurance.

Tax-deferred vs. tax-free: tax-deferred means investment income is not taxed in the year earned, but may be taxed upon a pre-death disposition (withdrawal, loan). If paid out at death as part of the death benefit, the accumulated investment income is paid tax-FREE and is never subject to tax.
Non-exempt policies: fail the exempt test and are subject to ANNUAL ACCRUAL TAXATION - the policyholder must report investment income earned within the policy as taxable income each year it is earned, regardless of whether he receives it.

📐 The MTAR rule: keeping the policy exempt

The Maximum Tax Actuarial Reserve (MTAR) rule says the cash value of the investment account cannot exceed the MTAR, which is the projected cash value of an Exempt Test Policy (ETP) - a hypothetical benchmark policy with the same death benefit and life insured. The insurer compares actual cash values to the MTAR on every policy anniversary and reports to the CRA.

G3 ETP (policies after Dec 31, 2016): 8-pay endowment at age 90. The ETP receives deposits for 8 years, large enough to make the investment account equal the death benefit by age 90, growing at a minimum 3.5% assumed rate minus cost of insurance. The projected cash value must not exceed this MTAR line until age 90.
G2 ETP (policies Dec 2, 1982 to Dec 31, 2016): 20-pay endowment at age 85. Deposits over 20 years, growing at a minimum 4% rate minus cost of insurance, equal the death benefit by age 85. G3 policies generally allow LOWER maximum deposits than G2 to maintain exempt status.

🔧 MTAR remedies: restoring exempt status

If a policy fails the MTAR test on its anniversary, there is a 60-DAY grace period to fix it before the policy officially loses exempt status. Once declared non-exempt, it remains non-exempt FOREVER. The insurer (not the policyholder or agent) is usually responsible for ensuring the policy stays exempt.

Remedy 1 - Increase the face amount: up to 8% per year, which raises the MTAR line by the same percentage. Some UL policies do this automatically every year even without a failing test.
Remedy 2 - Withdraw from the investment account: reduces the cash value below the MTAR but may trigger a policy gain. Example - James: UL policy with ACB $226,000 and CSV $400,000. Must withdraw $10,000. Prorated ACB = ($10,000 divided by $400,000) x $226,000 = $5,650. Policy gain = $10,000 minus $5,650 = $4,350.
Remedy 3 - Side funds or shuttle account: most policies include a provision to automatically move excess amounts from the investment account into a taxable side fund. Funds move back to the investment account if the investment account later falls below the MTAR line.

📉 The anti-dump-in (250%) rule

A second rule prevents large lump-sum deposits to the policy after its 7th anniversary, and applies to BOTH G2 and G3 policies. Starting on the policy's 10th anniversary and every anniversary after, the investment account is compared to its value THREE YEARS PRIOR. If the current value is 250% or more of the value three years earlier, the anti-dump-in rule triggers.

Worked example - Dean: 7th anniversary cash value = $4,200. 10th anniversary cash value = $15,600. Ratio = ($15,600 divided by $4,200) x 100 = 371%. Since 371% exceeds 250%, the anti-dump-in provision applies.
What it does: resets the ETP's date to the third preceding anniversary, comparing the current cash value against a (typically lower) re-dated MTAR - making the policy more prone to failing the exempt test unless remedies are applied.
Problem for minimum-funded policies and the 2017 relief: if a UL policy is funded minimally in early years (common for young families), later large deposits can easily trigger the 250% rule. A relief measure introduced January 1, 2017 allows the investment account to grow by more than 250% if it is less than 15% of the ETP's investment account value (for G2) or 37.5% (for G3).

⚠️ If a policy becomes non-exempt

Becoming non-exempt is rare due to the built-in safeguards, but does happen. Situations that can cause failure include: PUAs (high CSV-to-coverage ratio), better-than-expected investment returns, extra deposits to participating policies, or extra deposits generally.

Deemed disposition: becoming non-exempt triggers a deemed disposition. Policy gain = CSV minus ACB. No actual money changes hands - the policyholder does NOT receive the CSV - but he must pay tax on the policy gain at his marginal rate regardless.
Annual accrual taxation going forward: once non-exempt, the policyholder must report investment income earned within the policy as taxable income annually, in the year it is earned.

🔁 Tax implications of replacing a policy

Replacing an existing policy involves putting the new policy in place FIRST, then cancelling the existing one (this order matters). Cancelling the existing policy is a policy disposition, triggering a policy gain to the extent CSV exceeds ACB.

G1 policy tax advantages at risk: G1 policies have a higher ACB (cumulative premiums only, no NCPI deduction), are not subject to exempt testing, and are not subject to annual accrual taxation. Replacing a G1 policy loses these advantages permanently. Replacing a G2 policy with a G3 policy can also have adverse tax implications.

🤝 Absolute assignments: the general rule and non-arm's length parties

An absolute assignment transfers all ownership, control, and rights under a policy to another person. As a general rule, if the assignment is to an ARM'S LENGTH party (unrelated parties acting in their own independent economic interests), the proceeds of disposition equal the transfer price, and the policy gain equals the transfer price minus the ACB immediately before transfer. The recipient acquires the policy with an ACB equal to the transfer price.

Non-arm's length exception: if the assignment is by gift or bequest, from a corporation, by operation of law, or to any other non-arm's length party, the proceeds of disposition are deemed to equal the CSV less outstanding loans (what the policyholder would have received if he had fully surrendered). The recipient acquires the policy with an ACB equal to that same deemed amount. This prevents avoiding a policy gain by transferring at a discount.
Worked example - Robert and Jim: Robert (terminally ill) assigns his UL policy on his wife Joanna's life to his brother Jim. ACB = $34,000, CSV = $61,000. Policy gain = $61,000 minus $34,000 = $27,000. Jim acquires the policy with an ACB of $61,000.

💑 Spousal rollover

The Income Tax Act allows property to roll over from one spouse to another WITHOUT triggering a taxable disposition - this applies to life insurance. The transferring policyholder is deemed to have received proceeds equal to his ACB (policy gain = $0). The spouse acquires the policy with that same ACB. The rollover is AUTOMATIC.

Worked example - Noah and Eve: Noah assigns a policy (CSV $85,000, ACB $32,000) to Eve. Deemed proceeds = $32,000 = ACB. Policy gain = $0. Eve acquires with ACB of $32,000.
Opting out: the transferring spouse can opt out by filing a special election with his tax return. This might make sense if he has a lower marginal tax rate than his spouse, or if he has losses (rental, partnership) that can absorb the resulting policy gain. Opting out gives the recipient-spouse a HIGHER ACB (equal to the actual CSV), reducing her future policy gain. Example: Noah has a $70,000 rental loss. He opts out: policy gain = $85,000 minus $32,000 = $53,000, offset by the rental loss (no tax). Eve's ACB becomes $85,000 instead of $32,000.
Income attribution: when a taxpayer transfers property to his spouse, any property income (interest, dividends, rents) or capital gains earned on that property is generally attributed BACK to the transferor-spouse while he is still alive - taxable to him even though earned by the recipient-spouse. Attribution ceases upon the transferor-spouse's death.

👶 Child rollover

A policyholder can roll over an insurance policy to a child (including grandchild, great-grandchild, or a person wholly dependent on and in the custody of the policyholder before age 19) with NO deemed disposition, provided ALL conditions are met: (1) the policyholder receives no consideration for the transfer, (2) the life insured is the child (not the policyholder), and (3) the transfer is made DIRECTLY to the child, not via a trust or estate. Ownership cannot be transferred until the child is old enough to enter into a contract (generally age 16, varies by province).

Worked example - Rhonda and Jolene: Rhonda gives a UL policy (ACB $16,000, CSV $29,500) on Jolene's life to Jolene (age 19). Rollover applies: Rhonda's deemed proceeds = $16,000 (her ACB), policy gain = $0. Jolene acquires with ACB of $16,000. Jolene surrenders: gain = $29,500 minus $16,000 = $13,500, taxable to Jolene (she is over 18).
Income attribution for under-18 children: if the child disposes of the policy before age 18, income attribution applies and the policy gain is taxable to the ORIGINAL policyholder, not the child.
Intergenerational strategy - Dorothy, Madelyn, and Jackie: Dorothy (44% marginal rate) buys a UL policy on granddaughter Jackie's life, builds the CSV, then gives the policy to Jackie at age 18 (rollover applies; ACB = $18,000, CSV = $54,000). Jackie withdraws $15,000 to pay for university. Prorated ACB = ($15,000 divided by $54,000) x $18,000 = $5,000. Policy gain = $15,000 minus $5,000 = $10,000. Jackie has no other income - the gain is sheltered by her basic personal exemption and she pays NO tax. Dorothy would have paid $4,400 (= $10,000 x 44%) on the same withdrawal.

⚰️ Death of the policyholder

If the policyholder dies and is NOT the life insured, he is generally deemed to have disposed of the policy immediately prior to death. By default this triggers a policy gain on his final tax return equal to CSV minus ACB. Death is treated much like an absolute assignment.

Worked example - John and Jacinta: John owned a whole life policy on Jacinta's life (CSV $73,000, ACB $20,400). Left everything to his estate. Policy gain = $73,000 minus $20,400 = $52,600, taxable on his final return.
Spousal rollover on death: if John had left the policy to Jacinta, the rollover would apply - deemed proceeds = ACB of $20,400, policy gain = $0. Jacinta acquires with ACB of $20,400.
Contingent policyholder: a person named to automatically receive ownership upon the original policyholder's death, bypassing the estate (avoiding probate fees and delays on that asset). However, designating a contingent policyholder does NOT avoid the deemed disposition rules - the policy gain is still reported on the original policyholder's final return unless a rollover applies. Example: John designates his brother Maxwell as successor owner: Maxwell gets the policy directly, but the $52,600 gain is still taxable on John's final return.
Child rollover on death: to use the child rollover on death, the policyholder should name the child as SUCCESSOR OWNER. The transfer must be direct - it cannot go through a trust or estate.

🏛️ Using the policy as collateral and deducting premiums

Instead of a policy loan, a policyholder can use the policy's CSV as collateral for a loan from a THIRD-PARTY lender through a partial assignment. Key advantages: (1) a partial assignment is NOT a deemed disposition (no policy gain triggered), and (2) the full cash value stays in the policy, continuing to grow tax-sheltered.

Premium deductibility for business loans: when a business loan is secured by a partial assignment, some or all of the premiums may be DEDUCTIBLE if the loan is from an authorized lender and the lender requires the partial assignment. The deductible amount is limited to the LESSER of the NCPI and the premiums actually paid. If coverage exceeds the loan amount, the deduction is prorated.
Worked example - Heloise: borrows $200,000, secured by partial assignment of her $500,000 UL policy. Annual premiums = $12,000, NCPI = $3,200. Coverage ($500,000) exceeds loan ($200,000): deductible % = $200,000 divided by $500,000 = 40%. Deduction = 40% x $3,200 = $1,280.

📈 Annuitizing the CSV

A policyholder can use his entire CSV to purchase an annuity providing ongoing income payments (for life or a fixed period). Annuitizing the CSV is essentially a full surrender - it cancels the contract and triggers a policy gain to the extent CSV exceeds ACB.

Worked example - George: $500,000 policy, CSV $430,000, ACB $290,000. He buys a life annuity. Policy gain = $430,000 minus $290,000 = $140,000, taxable at his marginal rate.
If totally and permanently disabled at annuitization: the policy gain can be SPREAD OUT OVER THE LIFE of the annuity, easing the cash-flow burden and potentially reducing the marginal tax rate applied over time.
Partial annuitization: annuitizing only a PORTION of the CSV is treated as a partial surrender with a prorated policy gain.

🎯 The insured retirement strategy

Retired policyholders wanting a tax-free income source can negotiate a series of annual LOANS secured by the policy's CSV. Each loan is not taxable income (a loan is not income). When the life insured dies, the lender recovers the cumulative loans from the DEATH BENEFIT, with the remainder going to beneficiaries tax-free.

Worked example - Doris: UL policy with CSV of $380,000. She arranges $20,000 per year in loans, secured by the policy. Each payment is not reported as taxable income.
Key benefits: helps those minimizing net income to reduce taxes and avoid OAS clawbacks. The cash value continues to grow tax-sheltered within the policy. The lender will not allow cumulative loans to exceed a certain percentage of the CSV, which is why growth within the policy helps sustain the income stream.
Interest: may be paid annually (reducing cash flow) or capitalized (added to the cumulative loan balance and payable at death).

❤️ Charitable giving: the Charitable Donation Tax Credit

Three ways to use life insurance for charitable giving: (1) assign a new policy to a charity, (2) assign an existing policy to a charity, or (3) name a charity as the beneficiary. Each has different tax treatment. The Charitable Donation Tax Credit is non-refundable: federal rates are 15% on the first $200 of donations and 29% on amounts over $200. Provincial credits vary (4% to 20%). Unused credits can be carried forward up to 5 years. The annual claim limit is 75% of net income (rising to 100% in the year of death and the immediately preceding year).

Worked example - Mahmoud (New Brunswick, provincial rates 9.4% and 17.95%): donates $2,000. Federal: ($200 x 15%) + ($1,800 x 29%) = $30 + $522 = $552. Provincial: ($200 x 9.4%) + ($1,800 x 17.95%) = $18.80 + $323.10 = $341.90. Total credit = $893.90.

🆕 Assigning a new or existing policy to a charity

Assigning a NEW policy to a charity: because a new policy has no CSV, the assignment itself is NOT considered an immediate charitable donation and no tax receipt is issued. However, if the original policyholder continues to pay premiums AFTER assigning the policy, each premium IS a gift eligible for a tax receipt and the Charitable Donation Tax Credit (applies to both term and permanent insurance). Example - Thorsten: assigns his new $500,000 whole life policy to a charity, then pays $3,500 per year in premiums - he claims the full $3,500 as a charitable donation each year.

Assigning an EXISTING policy: if it is a permanent policy, the charity issues a tax receipt equal to the CSV (or fair market value) at assignment - because the charity can immediately surrender it for cash. An existing term policy has no CSV, so no tax receipt. Importantly, assigning an existing policy IS a deemed disposition - it triggers a policy gain (CSV minus ACB) taxable at the donor's marginal rate, even though it is a gift.
Worked example - Samuel (Manitoba, provincial rates 10.8% and 17.4%): assigns a $200,000 policy (CSV $36,000, ACB $10,000) to a charity. Tax receipt = $36,000. Policy gain = $36,000 minus $10,000 = $26,000. Tax payable = $26,000 x 44% = $11,440. But total Charitable Donation Tax Credit = $16,662.80, more than offsetting the tax. Net benefit: the tax credit exceeds the tax on the policy gain.

🎗️ Naming a charity as the beneficiary

The policyholder names the charity as the beneficiary without giving the charity ownership. NO tax receipt is issued when the charity is named, and NO tax receipt is issued for premiums paid while the policy is in force - because the charity does not own or control the policy, cannot surrender it, and there is no guarantee the policyholder will not change the beneficiary or let the policy lapse.

When the tax receipt IS issued: the charity issues a tax receipt for the death benefit paid out upon the life insured's death. The executor of the estate can allocate the available donation credit across the estate taxation year when the donation was made, a prior taxation year during estate settlement, and the policyholder's last two taxation years.

🗂️ Managing taxes upon death: four key liabilities

Life insurance is widely used to manage or pay for taxes arising at death. It cannot AVOID these taxes, but it provides the FUNDS to pay them, preserving the estate for intended beneficiaries. The four primary taxes or fees that can burden an estate are: (1) tax on capital gains, (2) tax from collapsing registered plans (RRSP, RRIF, etc.), (3) taxes payable by the estate, and (4) probate fees.

Probate fees: charged against assets by all provinces upon death EXCEPT Manitoba (court service fees) and Quebec (will probating fees). Naming a beneficiary directly or a contingent policyholder allows those policy proceeds to BYPASS the estate entirely, avoiding probate fees and delays on those amounts.
The agent's role: agents must estimate these liabilities with sufficient accuracy to determine whether the estate will cover them or whether insurance is needed to fill any gap.
