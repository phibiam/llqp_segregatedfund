Chapter 2 — Insurance Policy Provisions

When a life insurance company enters into a contract of insurance, it issues a policy. This chapter covers the parties to a policy, how policies are formed, the terms and termination of policies, assignment, and the specific provisions of individual life insurance, group insurance, accident and sickness insurance, annuities, segregated funds, and pension products.

👥 Parties to an individual policy

Insurer: the life insurance company that takes on the risk and issues the policy. The insurer responsible for the policy may change over years due to corporate purchases, amalgamations, or acquisitions. Acquiring insurers who assume the obligations of the former insurer must issue certificates of assumption to affected policyholders.
Policyholder (insured/owner): the individual or legal person who has legal ownership of the policy and exercises the contractual and statutory rights that go with ownership. The policyholder can also be the life insured, or can own insurance on the life of someone else (spouse, child, parent). There can be more than one policyholder (co-owners). Two policyholders may also own different portions of a policy.
Successor policyholder: if the policyholder is not the life insured and dies before the insured, ownership transfers to the person identified in advance as successor policyholder (also called contingent policyholder, successor-owner, or subrogated owner). If no successor is named, the new owner will be the policyholder's estate. This is designated in the policy or by an amendment.
Life insured: the person whose life is insured and on whose death the benefit is paid. Multiple individuals may be insured under one policy. For life insurance to be valid at issue, the policyholder must have an insurable interest in the life of the life insured.
Beneficiary: a person to whom or for whose benefit insurance money is payable. Beneficiaries have rights under the insurance policy but are NOT parties to it. The ability to designate a beneficiary is enabled by provincial or territorial insurance statutes. Most insurers now accept electronic beneficiary designations.
Preferred (family class) beneficiaries: designating these beneficiaries protects not just the death benefits but the policy itself from execution and seizure. They are: spouse (including common law spouse); child; grandchild; and parent of the life insured. Insurers will pay benefits based on the most recent beneficiary designation on file and are protected by law when doing so.
Irrevocable beneficiary designations: a policyholder may make a designation irrevocable — the declaration must be filed with the head office of the insurer (otherwise it remains revocable). If named irrevocably, the beneficiary's consent is required to: change or revoke the designation; withdraw policy cash; pledge or assign the policy; take policy loans; or surrender the policy. Even an irrevocable beneficiary outside the protected family class renders the policy exempt from execution of judgment and seizure. Loss of protection applies when designations are made to defeat, delay, or hinder creditors.
Restriction in legislative definition of beneficiary: provincial insurance legislation defines beneficiary as a person other than the insured or the insured's personal representative (executor). The policyholder or his estate does NOT qualify as a beneficiary under the relevant Insurance Act. Proceeds payable to the estate are collected by the executor and distributed according to the will. It is the relationship between the LIFE INSURED and the beneficiary (not the policyholder and beneficiary) that is crucial in common law jurisdictions for creating creditor protection.
Contingent (secondary) beneficiary: named to address the possibility that the primary beneficiary might die before the life insured. If there is no valid primary or contingent beneficiary designation in effect on the death of the life insured, the insurance proceeds will be paid to the current policyholder or to its estate.

👨‍💼 Group insurance policies

Plan sponsor: the employer, union, professional association, or other entity representing the group. The sponsor (called the group insured or group policyholder) enters into the master contract/policy with the insurer. In accordance with normal contract law, it is the group policyholder who determines through the policy provisions what the insurance benefits will be for members of the group.
Group life insured: under group policies, the person or member whose life is insured. Coverage typically includes members (participants or enrollees) and their families. Basic coverage for employees may be mandatory; optional increased coverage is subject to individual underwriting.
Anti-selection prevention: one reason membership in an employee group plan is mandatory is to prevent anti-selection of risks. Enrolling must happen within a defined short time after gaining employee status. Coverage changes are usually limited to an annual event or life changes (marriage, births, adoptions).
Group beneficiaries: under provincial insurance legislation, the group life insured may designate beneficiaries to receive the insurance benefit for which they are the life insured, even though not parties to the group master policy.

📝 Formation of an individual insurance contract

Offer and acceptance in insurance: completing an insurance application by an individual is generally considered an offer (the individual is the offeror). Under Canadian common law, it is the insurer's tender of the policy which constitutes the offer, and acceptance takes place when the applicant decides to take (receive) the policy. Both delivery of the policy AND payment of the initial premium are required before a life insurance contract is effective.
Application and representation of risk: the application form completed by the applicant with the agent's assistance is a critical part of forming the policy. The agent must NOT paraphrase or change the wording of insurer questions. All answers on the application form and any additional documents constitute representations to the insurer, which it relies upon during underwriting. Applicants and proposed life insured warrant accuracy and completeness by signing the application. The answers also serve as the benchmark against which changes in insurability are later measured.
Temporary insurance: an applicant may obtain temporary coverage during underwriting if they can answer no to three or four health questions. This provides limited insurance coverage, typically for 90 days. It will expire once the policy applied for is approved or declined. The amount available is typically the lesser of the face amount applied for or a maximum limit set by the insurer.
Changes in insurability: if there has been a change in insurability between application and policy delivery, the policy does not take effect, even if it was delivered and the first premium taken. Both the policyholder and the proposed insured person have a duty to disclose any change in insurability within their knowledge, from application until the policy is delivered. This applies whether the change was known or not.
Delivery of policy: the policy is NOT in force until all delivery requirements have been obtained and the first premium paid. If a premium was submitted with the application and the application is accepted, the insurance comes into force on an effective date determined by the insurer. Sometimes a policy is backdated to conserve a younger age for premium calculation — the applicant must also pay premiums for the backdated coverage.

🔚 Term and termination of policy

10-day free look (rescission): CLHIA Guideline G10 calls for a 10-day free look in the case of individual life insurance and individual accident and sickness insurance contracts. The policyholder can rescind an insurance contract within 10 days of signing it or accepting delivery, without penalty and with a full refund of premiums paid. For individual annuity contracts relating to segregated funds, there is a 2-business-day rescission right under CLHIA's Guideline G2.
Surrender: a policyholder can surrender a policy at any time, resulting in complete termination of rights for both policyholder and beneficiary. If the policy has cash surrender values (CSV), those will be paid out subsequently. Even a renewable term policy may have unused/unearned premiums which insurers will return.
Expiry or termination: a term life insurance policy may simply terminate in accordance with its terms. Guaranteed renewable and convertible term policies have age limits — they cannot be renewed after a specified age, and cannot be converted to permanent insurance after a specified age.

🚫 Termination by the insurer

Fraud, misrepresentation, or intentional concealment: fraud involves deliberate misstatement, deliberate omission of a material fact, or obtaining insurance for which the policyholder does not qualify. Unlike misrepresentation and concealment, fraud includes an intent to mislead the insurer. When fraud is involved, the incontestability period does NOT apply — the insurer may cancel at any time.
Incontestability period (two years): if a misrepresentation or concealment (other than age) made in good faith is discovered within the first two years, the insurer may cancel. After the two-year period, the insurer can NO longer cancel for misrepresentation or concealment made in good faith. However, fraud can be used to cancel at any time regardless of the contestability period.
Misstatement of age: the Insurance Acts contain statutory protection against termination for misstatement of age. The insurer would be entitled to amend the contract and adjust coverage to match premiums paid (or adjust premiums to the correct amount for the true age).
Non-payment of A&S premiums: the policy stays in force until a termination notice is sent. The insurer may terminate by mailing a pre-paid, registered, written notice to the last known address of the policyholder. Coverage ends after 10 days' written notice measured from the day after it is mailed.
Non-payment of life insurance premiums: any term life policy or permanent life policy with no cash values will terminate, subject to a 30-day grace period. Reinstatement is available if: the client applies within two years of the cancellation; and the insurer determines the insured still meets the insurability conditions of the cancelled contract.

🔄 Assignment of a policy

Absolute assignment: the transfer of ownership of the policy. The new owner (absolute assignee) has all the rights of the original policyholder, including the right to designate a new beneficiary or withdraw money. A change in ownership is a disposition for tax purposes and may result in taxable gains for the assignor. In some provinces, certain forms of buying and selling life insurance policies are considered trafficking and are prohibited (viatical settlements, life settlements, STOLI).
Collateral assignment: the policyholder assigns ownership of the policy to a lender as collateral for a loan. This restricts the policyholder from doing anything with their policy that could affect the value of the security. When the loan is paid, the collateral assignee releases their right and full ownership reverts to the original policyholder. If the life insured dies, the lender is only entitled to enough to retire the loan balance — the remainder belongs to the insurance beneficiary.

📄 Individual life insurance provisions

Policy particulars page: summarizes the name of the insured and life insured; amount of insurance proceeds and conditions; premium amount and grace period; participation in surplus or profits; conditions for reinstatement if lapsed; and options for surrendering for cash, obtaining a loan, or obtaining paid-up or extended insurance.
Statutory conditions: all policy terms and conditions must be set out in full when the policy is issued. The entire agreement consists of: the application; the policy; any document attached when issued; and any amendment (rider, endorsement, or addendum) agreed to in writing after issue.
Reduction vs. exclusion: a reduction is a contractual decrease in the death benefit amount at stated ages — it does not affect the insurer's liability, only the amount to be paid. An exclusion is a change to the coverage provided — death caused by an exclusion (e.g., underwater diving, flying as a pilot) results in no policy benefits.
Exclusions imposed by law: in addition to contractual exclusions, courts may deny entitlement to insurance proceeds when the beneficiary or insured caused or ran the very risk insured against, or when it is against public order (e.g., a beneficiary who murdered the life insured is disqualified from receiving the death benefit).
Pre-existing condition exclusions: more common in disability insurance than life insurance. A pre-existing condition that was not previously causing disability may still result in exclusion of disability benefits if a new disability is related to that condition.
Suicide clause: most individual policies restrict paying the death benefit if death is caused by suicide within the first two years that the policy is in effect or within two years of any reinstatement. After this stipulated period (typically two years), the death benefit is payable even if death is due to suicide.
Living benefits: some policies entitle the policyholder to receive an advance payment of a portion of the death benefit if the life insured is diagnosed with a terminal illness (usually death expected in less than two years). Some policies also allow withdrawing cash values on a tax-free basis if the life insured is totally disabled.
Cash surrender value (CSV): some permanent insurance policies provide for buildup of cash values on either a contractually guaranteed basis or a non-guaranteed variable basis. The CSV is paid out if the policy is cancelled — it is reduced by outstanding policy loans, unpaid premiums, or surrender charges.
Policy loans vs. collateral loans: a collateral loan from a third party has no size limit (apart from lender underwriting criteria) and is typically a percentage of the CSV. In contrast, an insurance policy loan has limits created by taxation of life policy transactions — borrowing too much can trigger policy gains taxable as income to the policyholder.
Riders (additional benefits): with the agreement of the insurer, additional coverages can be added through documents called riders or additional/attached benefits. These may change existing benefits or provide additional coverages.

👨‍👩‍👦 Group life and health insurance provisions

Premiums not guaranteed: the costs of group coverages are reassessed annually based on group census data, averaged over membership. The premium calculation takes into account the insurer's claims experience (ratio between premiums collected and benefits paid). Premiums can be changed annually and are an annual subject of negotiation.
ASO plans: some large employers prefer to fund employee benefits themselves (self-insurance) and hire an insurer to process, adjudicate, and administer claims and payments. These administrative services only (ASO) plans are NOT insurance plans and are not covered by provincial insurance statutes. Since July 1, 2014, long-term disability plans for federally regulated employees must be insured.
Certificates: when members join an insured group plan, they have no contractual connection with the insurer. Instead, the insurer gives the employer certificates to deliver to group members containing individualized coverage information. Provincial insurance statutes prescribe the information that must be provided.
Booklets: plan sponsors may use booklets to explain group benefit coverages. These are only summaries — the group policy provisions govern in any dispute. Under CLHIA Guideline G3, when a group insured person leaves the group, they have (if under 65) a group conversion privilege to convert their group life insurance to an individual policy without underwriting within 31 days of termination of coverage.
Applicable law: the law of the province where a group life insured is residing when they become insured is the law that applies to the insurer's rights and obligations, and the rights and status of beneficiaries under the group coverage.
Termination of group coverage: almost all group insurance policies are renewable annually. Individual coverage terminates when a member is no longer a member of the covered group (i.e., terminates employment). Group benefits may seem less expensive than individual coverage but group plan members do not enjoy the portability and contractual guarantees available with individual policies.

🏥 Accident and sickness insurance provisions

AD&D (Accidental Death and Dismemberment): covers defined physical losses or death due to an accident. The definition of accident can be complex with many restrictions and exclusions (death from sickness, suicide, war, riot, alcohol/drugs, criminal offence, extreme sports, etc.). AD&D can provide at least partial protection to persons who are medically unable to qualify for traditional individual life insurance.
Disability insurance: can cover full and partial disability, as well as temporary or permanent reductions in the ability to earn income. The future income option (FIO) permits the policyholder to increase coverage to keep pace with increases in income without going through medical underwriting again. Coverage does not come into effect until the policy is delivered to an applicant who confirms continuing good health and the premium is collected.
Critical illness (CI): insurance against the risk of suffering a life-threatening illness. If the person is diagnosed with a covered critical illness and survives for a period usually of 30 days, the insurer pays a lump sum. Coverage can be obtained for up to 36 covered critical illnesses. Some policies include return of premium (ROP) on death, expiry, or policy surrender.
Long-term care (LTC): provides a benefit when the insured cannot safely and independently perform a number of daily living activities, or must be constantly supervised due to deterioration of mental functions. May provide a weekly or monthly benefit or reimburse expenses. Activities of daily living include: feeding, dressing, grooming, bathing, getting in/out of bed, using the toilet. All disability and LTC plans have a wait period before benefits are payable — usually 30, 90, 180 days or longer.
Effective date of A&S: accidental death or personal accident insurance can take effect quickly (sometimes immediately) since no medical underwriting is needed. Sickness insurance requires underwriting and follows a similar process and timeline to individual life insurance.
Termination of A&S: the policyholder may terminate at any time on giving written notice. If the policyholder stops paying premiums, the policy terminates for non-payment after the grace period — but UNLIKE life insurance, 10 days written notice is also required. For cancellable A&S policies, the insurer may only terminate after sending written notice and the period indicated in the policy expires. Non-payment of premiums in and of itself does NOT allow the insurer to terminate — the formalities must be followed.
Statutory conditions for A&S: unlike life insurance, there are statutory terms and conditions that must be included in every individual accident and sickness policy for it to be valid. The policy must contain the name of the insured and insured person, the amount and conditions of insurance money, the premium and grace period, reinstatement conditions, and the term of insurance or its commencement/termination dates.

💰 Annuities and registered products

Annuities: policies where the insurer agrees to pay a fixed amount to a payee over a specified period. A life annuity has duration determined by a person's life. A term certain or fixed-term annuity has payments for a defined period. Only companies authorized to conduct life insurance business can offer life annuities. Annuities issued by life insurance companies are treated as life insurance under provincial and territorial insurance acts.
Parties to an annuity: the insurer (debtor/annuity provider); the policyholder or annuity holder; the annuitant or life insured (the measuring life on which the duration is based); the annuity grantee (named recipient of actual annuity payments); and the designated beneficiary. For all registered contracts (RRSP, RRIF, LIF, LIRA, TFSA, etc.), the policyholder, annuitant, and payee are always the same person.
Structured settlements: a special kind of single-premium annuity — non-assignable, non-commutable, and non-transferable — with special tax treatment. Instead of a lump sum in damages, the court awards or parties agree to a customized stream of damage payments funded through a structured settlement annuity. The stream of payments does not result in taxable income to the recipient, provided designed in accordance with CRA's Interpretation Bulletin IT-365R2.
Segregated funds (IVIC): an individual variable insurance contract (IVIC) is an individual annuity contract related to segregated funds. Premiums are invested in segregated funds managed by the life insurance company. Values vary based on investment performance. These policies guarantee to pay at least 75% of what the policyholder paid to the plan, on death or maturity, even if investments are worth less. Governed by insurance legislation, not securities law. Instead of a prospectus, insurers must provide an information folder at point of sale. Other documents (Fund Facts, Key Facts) must also be remitted at point of sale. The CLHIA Guideline G2 applies to IVICs. Segregated funds are NOT guaranteed when purchased under a group annuity contract (Guideline G2 does not apply).
Pension products: defined benefit pension plans (DBPP) — the pensioner's entitlement is defined by a formula based on income earned and years of service, not financial performance. Defined contribution pension plans (DCPP) — benefits determined by member and/or employer contributions and investment performance. Pooled registered pension plans (PRPP) — a new kind for self-employed individuals or those without an employer-sponsored pension plan.
Registered products: TFSA — deposits not tax-deductible but income/growth is not taxed and withdrawals are tax-free; cumulative contribution room is $102,050 in 2025. FHSA (First Home Savings Account, since April 1, 2023) — for first-time home buyers, annual contribution limit $8,000, lifetime limit $40,000; qualifying withdrawals are non-taxable. RRSP — flexibility for unrestricted withdrawal at any time (subject to withholding tax); re-contribution not permitted. RRIF — retirement income payout plan with minimized withdrawals, no minimum withdrawals required until age 71. LIRA — contains funds transferred from a pension plan; generally unable to withdraw before age 55. LIF — type of retirement plan for pension fund transfers; minimum and maximum annual income amounts apply.
