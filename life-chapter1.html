Chapter 1 - Introduction to Life Insurance

This lesson covers everything in depth - the risk of death, mortality vs. probability of death, life tables, the financial impacts of premature death, estate planning needs, business impacts, and the four risk management strategies. Take your time and work through it like a textbook chapter.

⚖️ The certainty of death, the uncertainty of timing

Everyone is constantly exposed to the risk of death - every day, every minute. The probability of dying today is much smaller than it will be decades from now, but the risk never disappears. In insurance terms, the probability of dying at a specific age is called the mortality rate.

What insurers track: age, gender, family history, health, smoking status, and even job and income level all influence an individual's risk of death. Insurers group people with similar risk profiles and use historical mortality data on each group to estimate individual risk - covered further in Chapter 9 (Application and underwriting).

📊 Two ways to measure the risk of death

Life expectancy is the average number of years a person within a certain group, at a certain age, can expect to live from that age forward. It assumes the group's past mortality experience holds true in the future.

Probability of death is the statistical probability that a person within a certain group, at a certain age, will die before reaching his next birthday.

Worked example: for Canadian males who were 65 between 2020-2022, the median male in that group will live another 19.3 years - meaning 50% will live to age 84.3 or longer, while 50% will die before then. Separately, 1.116 out of every 100 of those same 65-year-old men will die before their 66th birthday - a 1.116% probability of death within the year.

📈 Reading a life table

Life expectancy and probability of death statistics are compiled for defined groups and presented in a "life table" (or "mortality table"). With the exception of the first few years of life, probability of death starts low and increases very slowly until around age 40 - then it increases more dramatically year over year due to age-related health problems like cardiovascular disease.

🧭 Applying mortality data to planning

How an agent uses mortality information depends on the specific need being addressed. For a retirement savings or income plan, the focus is on life expectancy - it helps determine the appropriate planning horizon (how long the money needs to last). For an insurance needs analysis, the agent may find it useful to educate the client on his probability of death, to help him understand the risk is real and needs to be managed.

💔 Loss of income

One of the most devastating financial impacts of death results from the loss of an income earner, particularly if he was supporting a family that relied on that income.

Example: Justin and Tanya, both 30, have three young children. Tanya stays home with the kids, so Justin is the sole income earner. If Justin died, the loss of his income alone would be devastating to the family's finances.

👶 Loss of caregiver

Even the death of a non-income earner can have a significant financial impact on survivors, particularly if the deceased took care of children or other dependent family members.

Continuing the example: if Tanya died instead, Justin - as the sole income-earner - could not afford to stop working to care for the children himself. Unless a family member could step in for free, Justin would have to hire someone to take on the caregiver role, a real financial cost most families underestimate.

💳 Debt repayment

When a person dies, one of the executor's (in Quebec, "liquidator's") first responsibilities is paying off outstanding debts - mortgage, car loan, credit card debt. A lender may extend the loan to a surviving spouse or beneficiary who inherits encumbered property, but usually only after confirming that person has sufficient income to make the payments. If the lender decides the default risk is too high, it will recall the loan, demanding payment in full.

Continuing the example: if Justin died, the bank is unlikely to extend the mortgage to Tanya since she isn't working - she would likely have to sell the family home to pay off the debt, on top of losing her partner.

🧾 Income taxes at death

The income tax liability arising upon death can seriously erode an estate. When a person dies, he is usually deemed to have sold all of his property at fair market value (unless a rollover applies), which can trigger a taxable capital gain. If the estate lacks enough money to pay the resulting tax, the executor may have to sell assets that were intended for the heirs.

Registered accounts: RRSPs and RRIFs are deregistered at death, meaning their full value is taxed (unless a rollover applies) at the deceased's marginal tax rate, which for the top bracket in 2024 ranged from 44.5% (Nunavut) to 54.0% (Nova Scotia) depending on province.

🏛️ Estate creation and common objectives

Clients contemplating their eventual death often want to leave something behind - to have "something to show" for their life. For someone just starting out, with little or no net worth, building an estate can seem impossible without the financial contribution of a life insurance policy.

ObjectiveWhat it addresses
Income tax owingThe estate pays tax on transferred property so heirs receive it free and clear, avoiding a forced sale (e.g. a cherished family cottage)
Education fundsEnsuring children can still attend university/college even if a parent dies - can be a significant total cost across multiple children
LegaciesA financial gift to someone special, e.g. helping a child with a future down payment or business start
Charitable givingSupporting a meaningful cause upon death, sometimes for tax benefits as well (see Chapter 7)

🏢 Business impacts of death

The death of a key employee or shareholder can devastate a business, sometimes resulting in its collapse (see Key person life insurance in a later chapter).

Example: when Vertical restaurant's star chef Luigi died suddenly, revenue dropped dramatically. It took the owner, Allen, nearly six months to find a comparable replacement chef, plus an expensive marketing campaign to win customers back - the financial strain nearly bankrupted the business, illustrating how dependent some businesses are on a single key individual.

🛡️ The four risk management strategies

Regardless of the type of risk, there are four general strategies for dealing with it, which can be used alone or in combination:

StrategyWhat it means
Risk avoidanceDeciding not to expose yourself to the risk at all
Risk reductionTaking action to reduce the probability or severity of the risk
Risk retentionAccepting exposure to the risk and its consequences if realized
Risk transferFinding someone else (an insurer) willing to assume the financial consequences if the risk occurs

🚫 Risk avoidance and premature death

Risk avoidance means simply not exposing yourself to a risk - like choosing public transit instead of driving to avoid car accident risk. Unfortunately, it is impossible to avoid the risk of death entirely; simply by living, everyone will eventually die. The real issue is whether death occurs earlier than anticipated.

Premature death is death at a younger age than predicted. Since death itself cannot be avoided, the other three strategies become essential for addressing this specific risk.

🏃 Risk reduction

If a risk cannot be avoided entirely, it may be possible to reduce its probability or severity - this is risk reduction. A driver can reduce car accident risk by obeying traffic rules and maintaining his vehicle. Similarly, a person can reduce his risk of premature death by maintaining a healthy lifestyle and avoiding hazardous activities.

Important limitation: risk reduction does not eliminate the risk of death entirely - it only reduces the probability of a premature death. Additional strategies are still needed to deal with the remaining risk.

🤲 Risk retention

Risk retention means accepting that you're exposed to a risk and will bear the consequences if it happens. It's most appropriate for risks of low severity - meaning a manageable financial impact, like declining an extended warranty on an appliance and accepting the small risk of paying for a repair later.

Why retention rarely fits death: the risk of death is usually considered a HIGH severity risk because of its potentially devastating financial impact. A client without sufficient financial resources to absorb that impact needs a different strategy.

🔄 Risk transfer and life insurance

Risk transfer means finding someone else willing to assume the financial consequences if the risk becomes real - in the case of premature death, that someone is a life insurance company, an entity specifically created to bear this kind of risk. By paying premiums during his lifetime, a person can prevent a financial catastrophe for his survivors in the event of premature death, in exchange for a death benefit.

Example: James and Mary are both concerned about car accident risk. James avoids the risk by taking public transit (avoidance). Mary keeps driving but chooses a high-safety-rated car and wears her seatbelt (reduction), while also purchasing life and auto insurance (transfer) - together illustrating how multiple strategies can combine to manage a single risk.

🧩 Putting it together: choosing the right strategy

Since death itself cannot be avoided, and risk reduction alone never eliminates the risk, most clients facing the high-severity financial risk of premature death will need either risk retention (only if they have sufficient resources to self-insure) or risk transfer through life insurance.

The agent's role: help the client honestly assess whether his financial resources are sufficient to retain the risk of premature death, or whether transferring that risk to an insurer - through an appropriate life insurance product - is the more responsible choice given his dependants and obligations.
